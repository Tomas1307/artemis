# MASA-DOC-002: Quetzal Module — Scientific Laboratory Manual

## Module Overview and Research Mission

The Quetzal module serves as the primary scientific laboratory of Kuntur Station, designed to facilitate advanced research in microgravity environments. Positioned at 408 km altitude in Low Earth Orbit, Quetzal supports a crew capacity of four, with Research Specialist Kai Nakamura and Research Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan assigned as primary personnel. The module's core function is to enable experimentation across multiple disciplines, including fluid dynamics, materials science, and biological studies, all of which require precise environmental control and continuous telemetry monitoring.

Quetzal's operational parameters are tightly regulated to maintain conditions conducive to both crew safety and experimental integrity. The module's systems—ventilation, heating, lighting, cooling, and filtration—are integrated to sustain a stable environment, with redundancy measures ensuring uninterrupted research activities. Unlike other modules such as Jaguar, which prioritizes life support and engineering functions, Quetzal is optimized for scientific output, with its twelve workstations configured to accommodate simultaneous experiments.

The module's research mission is supported by MASA-OPS-009 (Scientific Experiment Execution), which outlines the procedural framework for conducting experiments, including pre-experiment checks, real-time monitoring, and post-experiment data validation. This protocol ensures that all research activities adhere to MASA's stringent scientific standards, minimizing the risk of contamination or data corruption.

## Laboratory Layout and 12 Workstation Configuration

The Quetzal module is divided into twelve distinct workstations, each equipped with specialized instrumentation tailored to specific research disciplines. Workstations 1 through 4 are dedicated to fluid dynamics experiments, featuring high-resolution imaging systems and precision injection mechanisms to study microgravity fluid behavior. Workstations 5 through 8 are configured for materials science, incorporating furnaces capable of reaching high temperatures, as well as spectroscopic analysis tools for real-time material characterization. The remaining workstations, 9 through 12, are reserved for biological research, with each station equipped with incubators, microscopes, and containment units to support cell culture and microbial studies.

The layout of the workstations is designed to optimize crew efficiency and experimental workflow. Workstations are arranged in a circular configuration around the module's central axis, allowing researchers to move seamlessly between stations while maintaining access to shared resources such as the module's ventilation and filtration systems. This arrangement also facilitates collaboration, as crew members can easily monitor multiple experiments simultaneously.

Each workstation is equipped with dedicated power and data interfaces, ensuring that experiments can be conducted independently without risk of cross-contamination or interference. The power distribution system is designed to handle peak loads of up to 80% of rated capacity under normal conditions, with alerts triggered at higher thresholds to prevent overloads. This configuration aligns with MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing), which mandates continuous monitoring of power consumption to avoid disruptions to ongoing experiments.

## Scientific Equipment Inventory

The Quetzal module houses a comprehensive inventory of scientific equipment, each piece selected for its ability to operate effectively in a microgravity environment. Central to the module's fluid dynamics research are the high-speed cameras and laser Doppler velocimeters, which provide real-time data on fluid flow patterns. These instruments are calibrated to operate within the module's normal temperature range of 18.0°C to 26.0°C, ensuring optimal performance without thermal interference.

For materials science research, the module is equipped with a suite of furnaces and spectroscopic analyzers. The furnaces are capable of precise temperature control, with safety protocols in place to prevent overheating. Should temperatures exceed the normal range but remain below 35.0°C, a low-severity alert is triggered, prompting crew members to inspect the cooling systems. If temperatures continue to rise beyond 45.0°C, a medium-severity alert is issued, requiring immediate corrective action to prevent equipment damage.

Biological research in Quetzal is supported by a range of incubators, centrifuges, and containment units. These systems are designed to maintain strict environmental controls, with humidity levels kept within the normal range of 40% to 60%. Deviations outside this range, particularly in the low-alert bands of 30% to 39% or 61% to 70%, can compromise experimental integrity, necessitating adjustments to the module's filtration and ventilation systems.

## Telemetry Specifications and Operational Ranges

The Quetzal module's telemetry system is designed to provide real-time monitoring of critical environmental parameters, ensuring both crew safety and experimental accuracy. Temperature is maintained within a normal range of 18.0°C to 26.0°C, with deviations beyond this range triggering progressively severe alerts. A low-severity alert is issued when temperatures exceed 35.0°C, indicating a potential cooling system inefficiency. If temperatures rise further to 45.0°C, a medium-severity alert is triggered, requiring immediate intervention to prevent equipment failure. Temperatures above 60.0°C constitute a critical emergency, activating MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) and necessitating immediate evacuation of the module.

Pressure within Quetzal is maintained between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa under normal conditions. A drop in pressure to 95.0 kPa triggers a low-severity alert, prompting crew members to inspect the module's sealing and ventilation systems. Further reductions to 90.0 kPa or 85.0 kPa escalate the alert to medium and high severity, respectively, with the latter activating MASA-SEC-011 (Moderate Pressure Drop Response). Pressures below 85.0 kPa are classified as critical, triggering MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) and requiring immediate action to restore pressure integrity.

Oxygen levels in Quetzal are maintained between 19.5% and 23.5% to support both crew respiration and experimental requirements. A drop to 18.0% triggers a low-severity alert, indicating a potential issue with the oxygen generation system. Further reductions to 16.0% and 14.0% escalate the alert to medium and high severity, respectively, with the latter activating MASA-SEC-010 (Low Oxygen Warning Response). Oxygen levels below 14.0% are classified as critical, triggering MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response) and requiring immediate evacuation of the module.

Radiation levels within Quetzal are continuously monitored, with normal levels not exceeding 0.5 mSv/hour. A low-severity alert is triggered when radiation levels reach 1.0 mSv/hour, prompting crew members to verify shielding integrity. Further increases to 2.0 mSv/hour and 5.0 mSv/hour escalate the alert to medium and high severity, respectively, with the latter activating MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory). Radiation levels above 5.0 mSv/hour are classified as critical, triggering MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) and requiring station-wide protective measures.

Humidity within Quetzal is maintained between 40% and 60% to ensure optimal experimental conditions. Deviations into the low-alert bands of 30% to 39% or 61% to 70% trigger low-severity alerts, prompting adjustments to the module's filtration systems. Further deviations into the medium-alert bands of 20% to 29% or 71% to 80% escalate the alert to medium severity, requiring immediate corrective action. Humidity levels below 15% or above 90% are classified as critical, necessitating immediate intervention to prevent equipment damage or experimental compromise.

Power consumption within Quetzal is monitored to ensure that the module's systems operate within safe limits. Normal power usage is maintained below 80% of rated capacity, with low-severity alerts triggered when consumption exceeds 88%. Further increases to 93% and 97% escalate the alert to medium and high severity, respectively, with the latter activating MASA-SEC-018 (Power Fluctuation Response). Power levels above 97% are classified as critical, triggering MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) and requiring immediate load shedding to prevent system overload.

## Experiment Safety Protocols

Safety protocols in the Quetzal module are designed to protect both crew members and experimental integrity. All experiments are conducted in accordance with MASA-OPS-009 (Scientific Experiment Execution), which mandates pre-experiment safety briefings, real-time monitoring, and post-experiment decontamination procedures. Crew members are required to wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) when handling hazardous materials, with containment units in workstations 9 through 12 designed to prevent biological contamination.

In the event of a biological contamination incident, MASA-SEC-019 (Biological Contamination Containment) is activated, requiring immediate isolation of the affected workstation and decontamination procedures. This protocol ensures that any potential hazards are contained within the module, preventing station-wide exposure. Similarly, MASA-SEC-009 (Toxic Atmosphere Detection) is triggered in the event of a chemical spill or release, prompting crew members to evacuate the module and initiate decontamination procedures.

Fire safety is a critical concern in the Quetzal module, given the presence of high-temperature furnaces and electrical equipment. In the event of a fire, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) is activated, with crew members trained to respond swiftly to contain the fire and evacuate the module if necessary. Regular fire drills are conducted as part of MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill), ensuring that all crew members are prepared to respond effectively to fire emergencies.
