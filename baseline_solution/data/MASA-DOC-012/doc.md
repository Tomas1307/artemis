# MASA-DOC-012: Critical Response and Evacuation Protocols

## Protocol Overview and Command Authority

The MASA Critical Response and Evacuation Protocols establish the operational framework for managing high-severity incidents aboard Kuntur Station. These protocols are activated under conditions that threaten crew safety, station integrity, or mission continuity, with response actions escalating from module-specific containment to full station evacuation. Command authority during critical incidents follows a strict hierarchy: Commander Santiago Reyes retains ultimate decision-making responsibility, with delegation to Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza for medical emergencies and Engineer Pavel Kozlov for technical system failures. The Cóndor module serves as the central command hub during station-wide emergencies, while module-specific incidents are managed by the assigned specialist or engineer.

The protocols outlined in this document are designed to interface with existing operational procedures, particularly MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill), which ensures crew readiness through regular simulation of critical scenarios. All protocols are classified as either high-severity (module-specific) or critical-severity (station-wide), with the latter requiring immediate station-wide action. The threshold for critical-severity protocols is met when two or more critical systems fail simultaneously or when a catastrophic structural breach is confirmed, as defined in MASA-SEC-020 (Emergency Station Evacuation).

## MASA-SEC-005: Medical Emergency Response

MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) is activated when a crew member exhibits critical vital signs or when a medical emergency is reported by any station personnel. This protocol operates at high severity with module-only scope, meaning response actions are confined to the affected module while maintaining station-wide awareness. The trigger condition is deliberately broad to encompass any physiological crisis, including cardiac events, traumatic injuries, or acute illness, as defined by MASA-OPS-012 (Crew Medical Examination) standards.

Upon activation, the immediate priority is to notify Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza, who is stationed in the Tucán medical bay. The duty crew member must transmit an urgent message directly to Mendoza while simultaneously preparing the Tucán module for emergency medical operations. Non-essential crew movement is restricted to prevent interference with medical response efforts, and MASA ground control's medical team is contacted for real-time consultation. The protocol emphasizes rapid stabilization of the patient within the module before considering transfer to Tucán, as movement may exacerbate certain conditions.

The protocol's high-severity classification reflects the potential for rapid deterioration in microgravity medical emergencies. Unlike MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), which has a clear quantitative threshold (low oxygen), medical emergencies are qualitative by nature, requiring immediate human assessment. The protocol interfaces with MASA-OPS-012, which establishes baseline health metrics, allowing Mendoza to quickly determine deviation from normal parameters. Crew training under MASA-OPS-015 includes medical emergency simulations, ensuring all personnel can initiate the protocol even if Mendoza is temporarily unavailable.

## MASA-SEC-019: Biological Contamination Containment

MASA-SEC-019 (Biological Contamination Containment) is triggered exclusively by a positive detection from the biological contamination sensor in the Quetzal laboratory module. This high-severity, module-only protocol is critical due to the potential for experimental pathogens to compromise crew health or station biosafety. The Quetzal module, under the primary responsibility of Specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan, contains the station's most sensitive biological research equipment, making containment protocols essential.

Immediate quarantine of the contaminated sample area is the first required action, with no crew member permitted to re-enter without full personal protective equipment (PPE). The protocol mandates notification of Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza, who assesses potential exposure risks, and the halting of all active experiments in Quetzal to prevent cross-contamination. A high-alert signal is transmitted from Quetzal to both Cóndor command and MASA ground control, ensuring station-wide awareness without triggering unnecessary evacuation procedures.

The protocol's design reflects lessons from terrestrial BSL-3 containment facilities, adapted for microgravity conditions. Unlike MASA-SEC-009 (Toxic Atmosphere Detection), which addresses chemical hazards, biological contamination requires specialized handling to preserve sample integrity while protecting crew. The protocol interfaces with MASA-OPS-009 (Scientific Experiment Execution), which includes pre-experiment biosafety checks, and MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance), which ensures the Quetzal module's air filtration systems are operating at optimal efficiency to contain any airborne particles.

## MASA-SEC-020: Emergency Station Evacuation

MASA-SEC-020 (Emergency Station Evacuation) represents the most severe operational contingency, activated when two or more critical systems fail simultaneously or when a catastrophic structural breach is confirmed. This critical-severity, station-wide protocol supersedes all other response measures, requiring immediate action from all crew members. The threshold of two simultaneous critical failures was established based on redundancy analysis showing that single-system failures can typically be managed through module-specific protocols like MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) or MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression).

The protocol's required actions begin with the initiation of all-crew evacuation to the emergency capsule, located in the Cóndor module. Commander Santiago Reyes leads the evacuation process, ensuring orderly movement while Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza remains last to leave, verifying no crew members are left behind. The Colibrí module, under Pilot Ana Valdivia's control, transmits the MAYDAY signal to MASA ground control using redundant communication arrays. Mission-critical data is secured to portable storage devices during evacuation, with priority given to experimental results from Quetzal and system diagnostics from Jaguar.

The evacuation sequence is designed to minimize time from alert to capsule departure. Crew members don emergency suits capable of sustaining life for an extended duration, providing thermal protection and oxygen supply during descent. The protocol interfaces with MASA-OPS-003 (Docking and Undocking Procedure) for capsule separation and MASA-OPS-007 (Orbital Adjustment Maneuver) for emergency deorbit calculations. Unlike module-specific protocols, MASA-SEC-020 requires complete station abandonment, with no provision for salvage operations until ground control confirms safe re-entry conditions.

## Evacuation Capsule Capacity and Location

The primary evacuation capsule is housed in the Cóndor module, with a secondary capsule located in the Jaguar module as a redundancy measure. Each capsule is designed to accommodate all six crew members simultaneously, with life support systems capable of sustaining the full complement for the expected descent duration. The capsules are equipped with independent oxygen supplies, thermal regulation, and communication systems that interface with both MASA ground control and international search-and-rescue satellites.

The Cóndor capsule serves as the primary evacuation point due to its central location and direct access to the station's command systems. In scenarios where Cóndor is compromised, the Jaguar capsule provides an alternative, though access requires traversing through potentially affected modules. Both capsules undergo weekly system checks as part of MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), with particular attention to parachute deployment mechanisms and heat shield integrity. The capsules' locations were selected based on structural analysis showing these modules have the highest probability of remaining intact during catastrophic events.

## MAYDAY Communication Procedures

The MAYDAY signal transmission is the responsibility of Pilot Ana Valdivia operating from the Colibrí module. The signal is broadcast on all available frequencies, including MASA's dedicated emergency channel and international distress frequencies monitored by COSPAS-SARSAT. The transmission includes automated station telemetry data, crew manifest, and the nature of the emergency as determined by the activated protocol. Redundant transmission is maintained until ground control confirms receipt or until capsule separation is complete.

The communication protocol prioritizes brevity and clarity, with a standardized message format that includes station identifier (Kuntur), coordinates, and the specific protocol triggering evacuation (e.g., "MASA-SEC-020 activated"). This format ensures compatibility with international rescue protocols while providing MASA ground control with immediate situational awareness. The Colibrí module's communication arrays are hardened against electromagnetic interference, allowing transmission even during solar events that might trigger MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory).

## Crew Priority and Evacuation Order

The evacuation order is strictly defined to prevent bottlenecks at critical junctures. Commander Santiago Reyes leads the evacuation, ensuring the path to the capsule remains clear and coordinating with ground control during the process. Engineer Pavel Kozlov is responsible for verifying all non-essential systems are powered down to prevent electrical hazards during evacuation. Specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan secure experimental data from Quetzal, with priority given to biological samples that could pose contamination risks if left unattended.

Pilot Ana Valdivia maintains communication systems until the final moment, ensuring continuous MAYDAY transmission. Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza performs a final sweep of all modules, verifying no crew members are incapacitated or trapped. This order reflects both operational priorities and the specialized roles of each crew member. The protocol includes contingency measures for scenarios where a crew member is unable to evacuate, requiring the remaining crew to proceed while ground control assesses rescue options.

The evacuation order is practiced during MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill) simulations, with variations introduced to account for different module starting points and potential obstructions. The protocol emphasizes that no crew member should delay evacuation to retrieve non-essential items, as the capsules contain all necessary survival equipment. The strict adherence to this order has been validated through multiple simulation iterations, demonstrating optimal evacuation times while maintaining crew safety.
