# MASA-DOC-009: Radiation Safety Protocols

## Protocol Overview and Radiation Monitoring

Radiation monitoring aboard Kuntur Station is a continuous, multi-layered process that integrates real-time dosimetry, predictive solar activity modeling, and automated alert systems to ensure crew safety during both routine operations and solar particle events. The station's radiation detection network consists of multiple solid-state detectors distributed across all six modules, with redundant sensors in the Jaguar and Condor modules due to their designation as primary radiation shelters. Each detector samples ambient radiation levels at frequent intervals, transmitting data to the Condor command center where the Radiation Safety Officer—currently Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov—oversees trend analysis as part of MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check).

The operational radiation environment in Low Earth Orbit at 408 km altitude typically exposes crew members to background levels between 0.1 and 0.5 mSv/hour during quiet solar conditions. This baseline varies by module due to differences in shielding mass: the Jaguar module, with its thick aluminum-lithium alloy walls and additional polyethylene shielding, maintains the lowest ambient readings at a low level, while the Colibri module, optimized for observational transparency, registers the highest baseline at a higher level. The station-wide alert system employs three severity tiers: low-severity alerts trigger at a certain level, indicating elevated but manageable exposure; medium-severity alerts activate at a slightly higher level, marking the threshold where cumulative exposure begins to approach weekly limits; and critical-severity alerts, governed by MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol), engage when any module exceeds a high level—a level where acute radiation syndrome becomes a concern within a prolonged period of continuous exposure.

Radiation monitoring operates on a dual-track system: passive dosimeters worn by all crew members record cumulative exposure, while active sensors provide real-time ambient data. During each crew shift handoff under MASA-OPS-006, the outgoing Radiation Safety Officer must verify that all six crew members' dosimeters have been read and logged in the medical database maintained by Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza in the Tucan module. The station's radiation safety software automatically calculates remaining safe operating time for each crew member based on their cumulative exposure, with hard limits set at a certain threshold per 30-day period and a higher threshold per annual cycle—thresholds derived from MASA's adoption of the International Commission on Radiological Protection's recommendations for low-Earth orbit operations.

## MASA-SEC-004: Radiation Lockdown Protocol

MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) represents the station's highest-priority radiation response, activating automatically when any module's radiation sensor reports sustained readings exceeding a high level—a threshold carefully selected based on a radiation safety study which determined that exposure above this level for more than a certain duration would exceed the 30-day limit for even the least-exposed crew member. The protocol's station-wide scope reflects the fact that radiation events at this severity typically indicate either a solar proton event with high energies or a failure in the station's passive shielding integrity, both of which require immediate protective action across all modules.

Upon activation, the protocol enforces five mandatory actions in sequence: first, an automated station-wide alert broadcasts through both the audio system and crew personal devices, using a distinct radiation warning tone to ensure immediate recognition even during sleep periods. Second, all crew members must immediately proceed to either the Jaguar or Condor modules, which are designated as primary radiation shelters due to their enhanced shielding—Jaguar's shielding provides a significant reduction in dose rate compared to baseline modules, while Condor's command center location ensures continuous monitoring capability. Third, the protocol automatically cancels all Extravehicular Activity (EVA) operations, including those in preparation under MASA-OPS-004, by locking the airlock control systems and requiring manual override from the Condor command center. Fourth, the station's radiation shielding protocols activate, which includes extending the deployable polyethylene shielding panels on the solar-facing side of the station and reorienting the station to minimize exposed surface area—a maneuver that must be coordinated with MASA-OPS-007 (Orbital Adjustment Maneuver) if the event coincides with critical orbital phases. Finally, the protocol mandates continuous individual exposure monitoring, with dosimeter readings transmitted to the medical database every few minutes instead of the standard interval.

The protocol remains active until radiation levels in all modules fall below a certain level for a sustained period, at which point the Radiation Safety Officer may initiate a phased return to normal operations. During the lockdown, crew members are restricted to the shielded modules, with rotations limited to short shifts to prevent any individual from approaching their cumulative limits. The Condor module's command center becomes the operational hub, with Commander Santiago Reyes assuming direct control of all station systems to ensure coordinated response. Historical data from a solar maximum event demonstrates the protocol's effectiveness: during a significant spike that lasted several hours, the combination of sheltering and shielding reduced actual crew exposure to a safe level—well within established limits.

## MASA-SEC-012: Solar Flare Advisory

MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory) serves as the station's early warning system for elevated radiation conditions, activating when either the solar activity index reaches a high level—or when radiation levels in any module fall between a certain range. This medium-severity protocol represents a critical intermediate state where proactive measures can prevent the need for full lockdown while still protecting crew health. The lower threshold was established based on MASA's analysis of a solar particle event, which showed that exposure at this level for a certain duration would consume a significant portion of a crew member's 30-day allowance—a manageable portion that warrants increased vigilance.

The protocol's activation triggers a cascading series of protective measures beginning with the immediate cancellation or postponement of all scheduled EVA operations. This precaution reflects the fact that EVA suits provide only a fraction of the shielding effectiveness of the Jaguar module, making external operations particularly hazardous during elevated radiation periods. Crew members are then directed to relocate preferentially to the Jaguar and Condor modules, though unlike the full lockdown protocol, movement between modules remains permitted for essential operations. The station-wide medium alert employs a distinct auditory pattern to differentiate it from critical alerts, and all crew members receive notification through their personal devices with specific instructions based on their current location.

Radiation monitoring frequency increases to frequent intervals under this protocol, with each reading automatically logged to both the engineering and medical databases. This heightened monitoring enables the Radiation Safety Officer to detect trends that might indicate an impending transition to critical levels. The protocol also mandates continuous logging of cumulative exposure for all crew members, with the medical database calculating remaining safe operating time in real-time. During a spring equinox solar event, this protocol demonstrated its value when a significant reading in the Quetzal module triggered the advisory, allowing crew to relocate before levels reached higher values—preventing what would have been a notable exposure for Specialist Kai Nakamura, who was conducting experiments in that module at the time.

The Solar Flare Advisory remains in effect until either the solar activity index drops below a certain level or radiation levels in all modules fall below a safe level for a sustained period. During this time, non-essential systems in high-radiation modules like Colibri may be powered down to reduce crew exposure during maintenance activities, and all crew members are required to wear their dosimeters in the external pocket of their flight suits for immediate reading access. The protocol's medium severity allows for continued scientific operations in shielded modules, though all experiments involving radiation-sensitive equipment must be approved by the Radiation Safety Officer before commencement.

## Shielded Module Designations (Jaguar, Cóndor)

The Jaguar and Condor modules serve as Kuntur Station's primary radiation shelters, each incorporating specialized shielding designs that reduce ambient radiation exposure by a significant amount compared to baseline modules. The Jaguar module's shielding system consists of a thick aluminum-lithium alloy primary layer supplemented with a layer of polyethylene—materials selected for their combination of structural integrity and hydrogen content, which effectively scatters high-energy protons. This configuration provides particular protection against solar particle events, which typically consist of high-energy protons that would otherwise penetrate standard module walls. The module's shielding extends to all critical systems, including the life support racks and crew quarters, ensuring that essential operations can continue even during prolonged radiation events.

The Condor module, while serving as the station's command center, incorporates a slightly different shielding approach optimized for both radiation protection and operational flexibility. Its thick aluminum primary layer is augmented with a layer of polyethylene, providing a notable dose reduction while maintaining the structural requirements for housing the station's primary control systems. The module's central location and redundant power systems make it the ideal operational hub during radiation events, as demonstrated during a solar maximum when Commander Santiago Reyes coordinated all station operations from Condor for an extended period while radiation levels in other modules exceeded safe thresholds. Both modules feature deployable shielding panels that can be extended during radiation events, adding an additional layer of polyethylene protection to solar-facing surfaces—a capability that reduces dose rates by an additional amount when fully deployed.

The shielding effectiveness of these modules becomes particularly critical during the station's passage through the South Atlantic Anomaly (SAA), where the Earth's magnetic field dips to its lowest altitude, allowing trapped radiation to penetrate to 408 km. During these brief transits, which occur several times daily, ambient radiation in unshielded modules can spike to higher levels—nearly double the normal background. Crew scheduling under MASA-OPS-006 (Crew Shift Handoff) accounts for these transits by ensuring that at least two crew members are always present in either Jaguar or Condor during SAA passages, with Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov typically overseeing the radiation monitoring from Jaguar's engineering station.

The modules' shielding systems require regular maintenance under MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), with particular attention to the polyethylene panels which can degrade under prolonged UV exposure. Quarterly inspections verify the integrity of all shielding components, and the deployable panels undergo functional tests every 90 days to ensure rapid deployment capability. During a maintenance cycle, engineers discovered a slight reduction in shielding effectiveness in one of Jaguar's panels due to micro-meteoroid impacts—a finding that led to the implementation of additional protective covers during high-risk orbital debris periods.

## Crew Exposure Tracking Requirements

Crew exposure tracking aboard Kuntur Station operates as a multi-tiered system that integrates real-time monitoring, cumulative logging, and predictive modeling to ensure all personnel remain within established safety limits. Each crew member wears a personal dosimeter that records exposure at frequent intervals, with data automatically transmitted to the medical database during each shift handoff under MASA-OPS-006. The station's radiation safety software maintains individual exposure profiles that calculate both short-term and long-term cumulative doses, with hard limits set at a certain threshold per 30 days and a higher threshold per year—thresholds that align with international standards for low-Earth orbit operations while accounting for Kuntur's specific orbital parameters.

The tracking system employs a color-coded alert system visible on all crew personal devices: green indicates exposure below a certain percentage of 30-day limits, yellow between a certain range, orange between a higher range, and red above a high percentage. When any crew member's exposure enters the orange zone, the system automatically notifies both the individual and Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza, who then conducts a detailed exposure review to determine if operational adjustments are necessary. During a solar particle event, this system proved its value when Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan's dosimeter entered the orange zone after a certain duration of work in the Quetzal module—a notification that prompted her immediate relocation to Jaguar and prevented her from exceeding the 30-day limit by a significant amount.

Exposure tracking becomes particularly critical during Extravehicular Activities, where the reduced shielding of EVA suits exposes crew members to dose rates significantly higher than in the Jaguar module. All EVA operations under MASA-OPS-004 require pre-approval from the Radiation Safety Officer, who calculates the maximum allowable duration based on real-time solar activity data and the crew member's cumulative exposure. The EVA suit dosimeters provide redundant monitoring, with data transmitted in real-time to both the EVA crew member and the Condor command center. During a solar array repair mission, this system enabled Commander Reyes to terminate the EVA early when solar activity unexpectedly increased, preventing Pilot Ana Valdivia from exceeding her 24-hour limit by a notable amount.

The station maintains comprehensive exposure records that include not only the dosimeter readings but also contextual data such as module locations, activity types, and solar weather conditions. This database enables post-event analysis that has led to several operational improvements, including the implementation of the "radiation shadow" scheduling system where crew members with higher cumulative exposure are assigned to shielded modules during high-risk periods. Quarterly exposure reviews conducted by Medical Officer Mendoza compare actual exposure patterns against predicted models, with discrepancies investigated to identify potential shielding degradation or unaccounted exposure sources.

## EVA Restrictions During Radiation Events

Extravehicular Activities represent the most radiation-intensive operations conducted aboard Kuntur Station, with EVA suits providing only a fraction of the shielding effectiveness of the Jaguar module's enhanced protection. This vulnerability necessitates strict EVA restrictions during all radiation events, with protocols that vary based on the severity of the radiation environment. Under normal conditions with radiation levels below a certain level, EVA operations may proceed with standard precautions, including pre-EVA exposure checks and real-time dosimeter monitoring. However, when MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory) activates due to either elevated solar activity index or radiation levels between a certain range, all scheduled EVA operations are immediately canceled or postponed, and any in-progress EVAs must be terminated at the next safe opportunity.

The cancellation threshold was established based on MASA's analysis of EVA suit performance during a solar particle event, which demonstrated that exposure at this level would consume a significant portion of a crew member's 30-day allowance during a standard EVA—a risk deemed unacceptable given the potential for unexpected duration extensions. During such advisory conditions, the only exceptions to the EVA prohibition are emergency repairs critical to station safety, which require explicit approval from both Commander Reyes and the Radiation Safety Officer. Even these emergency EVAs are limited to brief durations and must be conducted with enhanced shielding measures, including the deployment of portable radiation shields at the worksite.

When MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) activates at radiation levels exceeding a high level, all EVA operations become absolutely prohibited, with the airlock control systems locked to prevent accidental egress. This restriction reflects the extreme danger posed by such radiation levels: during a solar maximum event, a significant reading would have delivered a notable dose to an EVA crew member in just a certain duration—nearly the entire 30-day limit in a single operation. The protocol's activation also triggers automatic retraction of all external equipment and tools to minimize the need for post-event recovery EVAs, with the station's robotic systems handling any essential external operations during the lockdown period.

EVA restrictions extend beyond the immediate radiation event, with post-event protocols requiring a monitoring period before resuming normal EVA operations. This precaution accounts for the potential for delayed solar particle arrival, particularly from coronal mass ejections that may follow initial flare activity. The Radiation Safety Officer must verify that radiation levels have remained below a safe level for a continuous period and that solar activity indices predict stable conditions for the next day before approving any EVA resumption. During a spring equinox solar event, this post-event monitoring period prevented unnecessary EVA cancellations by identifying stable conditions between successive solar flares, allowing critical maintenance to proceed without exposing crew to elevated radiation levels.

The station's EVA planning process under MASA-OPS-004 now incorporates real-time solar weather forecasting, with the Radiation Safety Officer receiving updated predictions from MASA ground stations every few hours during periods of elevated solar activity. This integration has reduced unplanned EVA terminations by a significant amount since implementation, while maintaining crew exposure well below established limits. The most recent demonstration of this system's effectiveness occurred during a solar filament eruption, when advanced warning allowed the cancellation of a planned EVA several hours before radiation levels exceeded safe thresholds—preventing what would have been a notable exposure for Pilot Valdivia and Specialist Nakamura.
