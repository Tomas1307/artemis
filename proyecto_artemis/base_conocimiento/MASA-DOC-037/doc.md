# MASA-DOC-037: MASA Internal Regulations and Code of Conduct

## Regulatory Framework

The operational and behavioral standards governing Kuntur Station, orbiting at 408 kilometers above Earth, are established under the authority of the Misión Aeroespacial Sudamericana Avanzada (MASA), founded in 2028 and headquartered in Bogotá, Colombia. These regulations are designed to ensure the safety, efficiency, and scientific integrity of the current Cóndor-7 mission, which commenced on September 1, 2031. The framework is built upon international space law, MASA’s internal policies, and the lessons learned from previous missions, including Cóndor-2, Cóndor-3, Cóndor-4, Cóndor-5, and Cóndor-6. Compliance with these regulations is mandatory for all crew members, regardless of nationality or specialization, and applies uniformly across all modules: Cóndor, Quetzal, Jaguar, Colibrí, and Tucán.

The regulatory framework is structured to address the unique challenges of operating in Low Earth Orbit (LEO), where environmental factors such as microgravity, radiation exposure, and the isolated, confined environment of the station demand strict adherence to protocols. For instance, the Radiation Lockdown Protocol (MASA-SEC-004) is a critical component of this framework, designed to protect crew members from elevated radiation levels exceeding safe thresholds, a condition that necessitates immediate station-wide action. Similarly, the Rapid Decompression Response (MASA-SEC-001) and Oxygen Depletion Response (MASA-SEC-002) protocols are integral to maintaining the station’s habitability, reflecting the life-or-death stakes of operating in a vacuum.

The framework also incorporates the operational procedures that govern daily activities on Kuntur Station. Procedures such as MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check) and MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance) are not merely administrative tasks but are critical to the station’s longevity and the crew’s safety. These procedures are designed to preemptively identify and mitigate potential failures, such as those that could trigger the Power Grid Critical Failure (MASA-SEC-006) or Structural Integrity Alert (MASA-SEC-008) protocols. By integrating these procedures into the regulatory framework, MASA ensures that the crew’s actions are both proactive and reactive, addressing issues before they escalate to critical levels.

## Chain of Command

The chain of command aboard Kuntur Station is a hierarchical structure designed to ensure clear decision-making pathways, particularly during emergencies where rapid response is essential. At the apex of this structure is Commander Santiago Reyes, a 45-year-old Colombian with 18 years of experience, including previous missions Cóndor-2, Cóndor-3, and Cóndor-6. Commander Reyes is assigned to the Cóndor module, which serves as the station’s command center, and his authority extends across all modules and crew members. His primary responsibility is to oversee the execution of the mission’s objectives while ensuring the safety and well-being of the crew.

Directly reporting to Commander Reyes is Pilot Ana Valdivia, a 38-year-old Peruvian with 12 years of experience, including missions Cóndor-5 and Cóndor-6. Assigned to the Colibrí module, Valdivia is responsible for orbital navigation and communications systems, playing a pivotal role in maintaining the station’s position in LEO and ensuring seamless communication with MASA ground control. Her expertise is critical during orbital adjustment maneuvers (MASA-OPS-007) and communication window management (MASA-OPS-008), where precise timing and coordination are essential to avoid triggering protocols such as the Navigation Deviation Alert (MASA-SEC-016).

The station’s specialized functions are overseen by Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, a 42-year-old Argentine-Russian with 15 years of experience, including missions Cóndor-3, Cóndor-5, and Cóndor-6. Assigned to the Jaguar module, Kozlov is responsible for the mechanical systems and life support engineering that keep the station operational. His role is particularly critical in scenarios that could lead to the activation of the Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression (MASA-SEC-003) or CO2 Scrubber Failure Protocol (MASA-SEC-014), where his technical expertise is indispensable. Kozlov’s daily responsibilities include conducting the Daily System Health Check (MASA-OPS-001) and Scheduled Preventive Maintenance (MASA-OPS-002), ensuring that all systems are functioning within their operational parameters.

The scientific and medical aspects of the mission are managed by a team of specialists and the medical officer. Kai Nakamura, a 34-year-old Japanese-Brazilian with 8 years of experience, and Fátima Al-Hassan, a 31-year-old Venezuelan with 5 years of experience, are both assigned to the Quetzal module. Nakamura specializes in astrophysics and materials science, while Al-Hassan focuses on biology and environmental systems. Their collaborative work is essential for the execution of scientific experiments (MASA-OPS-009) and the monitoring of the station’s environmental systems. Meanwhile, Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza, a 37-year-old Chilean with 10 years of experience, is assigned to the Tucán module. Mendoza’s role is critical in maintaining the crew’s health, conducting regular medical examinations (MASA-OPS-012), and responding to medical emergencies that could trigger the Medical Emergency Response (MASA-SEC-005) protocol.

The chain of command is designed to ensure that each crew member understands their role and responsibilities, particularly during emergencies. For example, in the event of a critical power grid failure (MASA-SEC-006), Commander Reyes would coordinate the station-wide response, while Kozlov would focus on restoring power and Valdivia would manage communications with ground control. This structured approach ensures that decisions are made swiftly and efficiently, minimizing the risk to the station and its crew.

## Communication Protocols with Ground Control

Communication with MASA ground control is a cornerstone of Kuntur Station’s operations, ensuring that the crew remains connected to mission support and technical expertise on Earth. The Communication Window Management procedure (MASA-OPS-008) governs the scheduling and execution of these communications, which are typically conducted during predefined windows when the station is in optimal alignment with ground stations. These windows are critical for transmitting telemetry data, receiving updates, and conducting real-time consultations with specialists on the ground.

The primary responsibility for managing these communications falls to Pilot Ana Valdivia, whose expertise in communications systems ensures that data is transmitted and received without interruption. In the event of a communication blackout, the Communication Blackout Protocol (MASA-SEC-007) is activated, requiring Valdivia to initiate contingency measures to restore communication links. This protocol is designed to address scenarios where the station’s ability to communicate with ground control is compromised, potentially isolating the crew from critical support.

The content and frequency of communications are dictated by the operational needs of the station. For instance, during the execution of scientific experiments (MASA-OPS-009), real-time data may be transmitted to ground control for analysis, while routine system health checks (MASA-OPS-001) may be conducted independently by the crew. However, any anomalies detected during these checks, such as those that could trigger the Power Fluctuation Response (MASA-SEC-018) or Toxic Atmosphere Detection (MASA-SEC-009) protocols, must be reported immediately to ground control for further analysis and guidance.

The communication protocols also include provisions for emergency scenarios, where rapid and clear communication is essential. For example, in the event of a medical emergency (MASA-SEC-005), Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza would coordinate with ground control to receive guidance on treatment options and potential evacuation procedures. Similarly, during a structural integrity alert (MASA-SEC-008), Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov would relay technical data to ground control to assess the severity of the issue and determine the appropriate response.

## Crew Conduct Standards

The conduct standards aboard Kuntur Station are designed to foster a professional, collaborative, and safe environment for all crew members. These standards are grounded in the principles of mutual respect, teamwork, and adherence to operational protocols, ensuring that the crew can work effectively together in the isolated and high-stress environment of LEO. The standards apply to all crew members, regardless of their role or nationality, and are enforced through a combination of self-regulation, peer accountability, and formal disciplinary procedures.

At the core of these standards is the expectation that all crew members will conduct themselves with professionalism and integrity. This includes adhering to the operational procedures that govern daily activities, such as the Crew Shift Handoff (MASA-OPS-006) and EVA Preparation and Execution (MASA-OPS-004). These procedures are designed to ensure that all activities are conducted safely and efficiently, minimizing the risk of errors that could lead to the activation of critical protocols such as the Airlock Seal Failure Protocol (MASA-SEC-017) or Emergency Station Evacuation (MASA-SEC-020).

The conduct standards also emphasize the importance of clear and respectful communication among crew members. Effective communication is essential for coordinating activities, sharing critical information, and resolving conflicts that may arise in the confined environment of the station. For example, during the execution of the Docking and Undocking Procedure (MASA-OPS-003), clear communication between Pilot Ana Valdivia and the rest of the crew is critical to ensuring that the procedure is executed safely and without incident.

The standards also address the crew’s responsibility to maintain the station’s systems and environment. This includes adhering to the Life Support Maintenance (MASA-OPS-010) and Power Management and Load Balancing (MASA-OPS-011) procedures, which are essential for maintaining the station’s habitability. Failure to adhere to these procedures could lead to the activation of protocols such as the Water Recycling System Failure (MASA-SEC-015) or Biological Contamination Containment (MASA-SEC-019), both of which could have serious implications for the crew’s health and safety.

## Reporting and Documentation Requirements

The reporting and documentation requirements aboard Kuntur Station are designed to ensure that all activities, anomalies, and incidents are recorded accurately and comprehensively. These requirements are critical for maintaining the station’s operational integrity, facilitating post-mission analysis, and ensuring compliance with MASA’s regulatory framework. The documentation process is integrated into all operational procedures, ensuring that every action taken by the crew is recorded and can be reviewed as needed.

The primary responsibility for maintaining these records falls to each crew member, who is required to document their activities as part of their daily routines. For example, during the execution of the Daily System Health Check (MASA-OPS-001), Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov is required to record the status of all systems, noting any anomalies or deviations from normal operating parameters. Similarly, during the Crew Medical Examination (MASA-OPS-012), Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza is required to document the health status of each crew member, recording any medical issues or concerns.

The reporting requirements also include provisions for anomalies and incidents that could trigger the activation of critical protocols. For instance, in the event of a moderate pressure drop (MASA-SEC-011), the crew is required to document the circumstances leading to the anomaly, the actions taken to address it, and the outcome of those actions. This documentation is critical for post-incident analysis, allowing MASA to identify the root cause of the anomaly and implement corrective measures to prevent recurrence.

The documentation requirements extend to all aspects of the station’s operations, including scientific experiments, maintenance activities, and emergency drills. For example, during the execution of the Scientific Experiment Execution (MASA-OPS-009) procedure, specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan are required to document the setup, execution, and results of each experiment, ensuring that the data is accurately recorded and can be transmitted to ground control for analysis. Similarly, during the Emergency Drill (MASA-OPS-015), the crew is required to document their actions and the effectiveness of the drill, providing valuable feedback for improving the station’s emergency response procedures.

## Disciplinary Procedures

The disciplinary procedures aboard Kuntur Station are designed to address violations of the crew conduct standards, operational protocols, and regulatory framework. These procedures are structured to ensure that all crew members are held accountable for their actions, while also providing a fair and transparent process for addressing misconduct. The procedures are escalatory, with the severity of the disciplinary action commensurate with the severity of the violation.

The first step in the disciplinary process is a verbal warning, issued by the crew member’s immediate superior or by Commander Santiago Reyes. This warning is designed to address minor infractions, such as failure to adhere to the Crew Shift Handoff (MASA-OPS-006) procedure or minor lapses in professional conduct. The warning is documented in the crew member’s record, ensuring that there is a clear record of the incident and the corrective action taken.

For more serious violations, such as failure to adhere to critical operational procedures or repeated infractions, the disciplinary process escalates to a written reprimand. This reprimand is issued by Commander Reyes and is documented in the crew member’s permanent record. The reprimand outlines the nature of the violation, the corrective action required, and the consequences of further infractions. For example, failure to adhere to the Power Management and Load Balancing (MASA-OPS-011) procedure, which could lead to the activation of the Power Grid Critical Failure (MASA-SEC-006) protocol, would likely result in a written reprimand due to the potential severity of the consequences.

In cases of severe misconduct, such as actions that endanger the safety of the crew or the station, the disciplinary process may result in the crew member being relieved of their duties and confined to their quarters. This action is taken only in the most serious cases, such as deliberate violation of critical protocols like the Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression (MASA-SEC-003) or Rapid Decompression Response (MASA-SEC-001). The decision to relieve a crew member of their duties is made by Commander Reyes in consultation with ground control, ensuring that the action is both necessary and justified.

The final step in the disciplinary process is the potential termination of the crew member’s assignment to Kuntur Station. This action is reserved for the most egregious violations, such as actions that result in the activation of the Emergency Station Evacuation (MASA-SEC-020) protocol or other critical emergencies. The decision to terminate a crew member’s assignment is made by MASA’s leadership in consultation with Commander Reyes and is based on a thorough review of the incident and the crew member’s record. This action is taken only as a last resort, reflecting the severity of the violation and the potential risk to the station and its crew.

The disciplinary procedures are designed to ensure that all crew members understand the consequences of their actions and the importance of adhering to the station’s operational and conduct standards. By providing a clear and escalatory process for addressing misconduct, MASA ensures that the crew can work together effectively and safely, maintaining the integrity of the Cóndor-7 mission and the safety of Kuntur Station.
