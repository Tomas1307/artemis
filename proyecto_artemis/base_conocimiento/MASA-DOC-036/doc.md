# MASA-DOC-036: Kuntur Station — Complete Systems Overview

## Station Architecture and Orbital Parameters

Kuntur Station, operated by Misión Aeroespacial Sudamericana Avanzada (MASA), is a modular orbital facility positioned in Low Earth Orbit (LEO) at an altitude of 408 kilometers. This altitude was selected to balance atmospheric drag, which requires periodic orbital adjustment maneuvers under MASA-OPS-007, with the operational advantages of proximity to Earth for resupply missions and emergency evacuation scenarios. The station completes a high number of orbits per day, maintaining a consistent ground track that ensures regular communication windows with MASA’s primary ground station in Bogotá, Colombia, as governed by MASA-OPS-008. The current mission, Condor-7, commenced on 2031-09-01 and represents the seventh operational phase of the station since its initial deployment in 2028.

The station’s architecture is designed for modular redundancy, with each of the six modules—Condor, Quetzal, Jaguar, Colibri, Vicuna, and Tucan—serving distinct but interdependent functions. Structural integrity is maintained through a combination of rigid docking collars and flexible expansion joints, allowing for thermal expansion while preventing stress fractures. The station’s orientation is stabilized using a combination of reaction wheels and magnetic torque rods, with Colibri’s navigation systems providing real-time attitude adjustments to counteract orbital perturbations. Solar arrays, mounted on extendable booms, provide primary power, with secondary battery systems ensuring uninterrupted operations during eclipse periods, which last a significant portion of each orbit.

## Module Interconnection Map

The Condor module serves as the central hub of Kuntur Station, with all other modules radiating outward in a star-like configuration. Condor is directly connected to Quetzal via a pressurized corridor, facilitating rapid crew movement between the command center and the scientific laboratory. Jaguar, the life support backbone, is linked to Condor through a dual-hatch system, ensuring redundancy in case of a hatch failure—a critical design feature given Jaguar’s Class A redundancy classification. Colibri, the communications and navigation module, is positioned opposite Quetzal, with its long-range antenna array extending perpendicular to the station’s primary axis to minimize interference with other systems. Vicuna, the cargo and storage module, is docked to Condor’s aft port, with its external docking port aligned along the station’s velocity vector to simplify approach trajectories for resupply vessels. Tucan, the crew quarters and medical bay, is connected to Condor’s starboard side, providing direct access to the command center while maintaining isolation for medical emergencies.

This configuration ensures that no module is more than two hatches away from Condor, optimizing emergency response times. For example, in the event of a medical emergency in Quetzal, Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza can reach the module from Tucan in a short time, while Commander Santiago Reyes can coordinate the response from Condor. The interconnection map also dictates power distribution priorities, with Jaguar receiving the highest allocation due to its life support systems, followed by Condor and Colibri, which are critical for station control and communications.

## Station-Wide Systems Summary

### Environmental Control and Life Support Systems (ECLSS)

The Environmental Control and Life Support Systems (ECLSS) are distributed across multiple modules but are centrally managed from Jaguar, where Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov conducts daily telemetry reviews as part of MASA-OPS-001. Oxygen levels across all modules are maintained between 19.5% and 23.5% under normal conditions, with deviations below 18.0% triggering low-severity alerts. Should oxygen levels drop to 16.0%, a medium-severity condition is declared, requiring immediate corrective action from the duty engineer. At 14.0% or below, the situation becomes critical, activating MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), which mandates immediate evacuation of the affected module. This threshold is consistent across all modules, reflecting the physiological necessity of maintaining oxygen levels above 14.0% to prevent hypoxia and cognitive impairment in crew members.

Pressure is maintained between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa, mirroring sea-level conditions on Earth to minimize long-term health effects. A drop below 95.0 kPa triggers a low-severity alert, while pressures below 90.0 kPa are classified as medium-severity, requiring activation of backup pressurization systems. At 85.0 kPa or lower, MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) is initiated, with automatic hatch sealing and emergency oxygen mask deployment. The uniformity of these thresholds across modules ensures standardized response procedures, though Jaguar’s life support systems are designed to stabilize pressure more rapidly due to their Class A redundancy.

Temperature control varies by module function. Condor and Tucan, which house crew for extended periods, maintain temperatures between 20.0°C and 24.0°C and 20.0°C and 25.0°C, respectively, to ensure comfort and operational efficiency. Quetzal, as a scientific laboratory, operates within a broader range of 18.0°C to 26.0°C to accommodate experimental requirements, while Jaguar’s tighter range of 19.0°C to 23.0°C reflects the sensitivity of its life support equipment. Colibri and Vicuna, which house electronics and cargo, have the widest temperature tolerances, with Colibri ranging from 15.0°C to 30.0°C and Vicuna from 10.0°C to 35.0°C. Critical temperature thresholds are module-specific: Condor and Tucan trigger critical alerts at 55.0°C and 52.0°C, respectively, while Quetzal’s critical threshold is 60.0°C, and Jaguar’s is 50.0°C. Colibri and Vicuna, with their electronics and cargo, have higher critical thresholds of 65.0°C, reflecting their reduced crew occupancy. When temperatures exceed these thresholds, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) is activated, with automatic fire suppression systems deploying and crew evacuating the module.

Humidity is maintained between 40% and 60% across all modules to prevent both condensation, which can damage electronics, and excessive dryness, which can irritate crew respiratory systems. Low-severity humidity alerts are triggered between 30%-39% and 61%-70%, while medium-severity alerts occur between 20%-29% and 71%-80%. High-severity alerts are declared between 15%-19% and 81%-90%, with critical conditions occurring below 15% or above 90%. These thresholds are consistent across all modules, as humidity control is primarily a comfort and equipment preservation measure rather than a critical life support parameter.

### Power Distribution and Management

Kuntur Station’s power system is designed to operate at up to 80% of rated capacity under normal conditions, with alerts triggered as capacity approaches maximum. Low-severity alerts are issued at 88% capacity, indicating that non-essential systems should be powered down. Medium-severity alerts at 93% capacity require the activation of backup power systems and load shedding procedures under MASA-OPS-011. High-severity alerts at 97% capacity mandate the shutdown of all non-critical systems, including scientific experiments in Quetzal and non-essential lighting in Tucan. At or above 97% capacity, MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) is triggered, with station-wide scope, requiring immediate intervention to prevent cascading system failures. Power management is a shared responsibility, with Condor’s command center monitoring overall distribution, while Jaguar’s systems prioritize life support allocations.

### Radiation Monitoring and Protection

Radiation levels are continuously monitored, with normal conditions defined as exposure rates below 0.5 mSv/hour. Low-severity alerts are triggered at 1.0 mSv/hour, prompting crew to limit exposure time in affected areas. Medium-severity alerts at 2.0 mSv/hour require the activation of localized shielding and the suspension of non-essential activities. High-severity alerts at 5.0 mSv/hour initiate MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory), with crew relocating to shielded areas in Jaguar or Tucan. At or above 5.0 mSv/hour, MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) is activated, with station-wide scope, requiring all crew to retreat to the most heavily shielded modules—Jaguar and Tucan—until radiation levels subside. This protocol is critical for protecting crew from acute radiation syndrome, which can occur with exposures above 1000 mSv in a short period.

### Communications and Navigation

Colibri serves as the primary communications hub, with its long-range antenna array maintaining continuous contact with MASA ground stations. The module’s navigation systems provide real-time orbital data, ensuring that Kuntur Station maintains its designated altitude of 408 kilometers. Any deviation from the planned orbital path triggers MASA-SEC-016 (Navigation Deviation Alert), requiring immediate corrective action under MASA-OPS-007. Communication blackouts, while rare, are addressed under MASA-SEC-007 (Communication Blackout Protocol), which mandates the use of backup systems and, if necessary, the deployment of emergency beacons to ground stations.

## Emergency Systems Overview

Kuntur Station’s emergency systems are designed to respond to a wide range of potential hazards, from environmental failures to medical crises. The station’s modular design allows for isolation of affected areas, preventing cascading failures. For example, in the event of a fire in Quetzal, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) would activate, sealing the module’s hatches and deploying fire suppression agents. Crew members would evacuate to adjacent modules, with Condor serving as the primary coordination point for the response.

Medical emergencies are managed under MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response), with Medical Officer Mendoza leading the effort from Tucan. Depending on the severity of the incident, this may involve isolating the medical bay, deploying emergency medical equipment, or coordinating with ground control for additional support. The medical bay in Tucan is equipped with advanced diagnostic tools and a small pharmacy, allowing for immediate treatment of most conditions. For more severe cases, evacuation to Earth may be necessary, requiring coordination with MASA-OPS-003 for docking procedures.

Power failures are among the most critical emergencies, as they can disrupt life support systems. MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) is triggered when power capacity exceeds 97%, with station-wide scope. This protocol mandates the immediate shutdown of all non-essential systems, activation of backup power sources, and, if necessary, the initiation of emergency evacuation procedures. Engineer Kozlov plays a central role in these scenarios, working from Jaguar to stabilize power distribution and restore critical systems.

Radiation events, such as solar flares, are managed under MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol). This protocol requires all crew to relocate to the most shielded modules—Jaguar and Tucan—until radiation levels drop below 5.0 mSv/hour. During these events, non-essential activities are suspended, and crew movement is restricted to minimize exposure. The protocol remains in effect until ground control confirms that radiation levels have returned to safe thresholds.

In the event of a structural integrity alert, such as a micrometeoroid impact, MASA-SEC-008 (Structural Integrity Alert) is triggered. This protocol involves sealing off affected areas, deploying repair teams if possible, and coordinating with ground control for further instructions. Depending on the severity of the damage, this may lead to the activation of MASA-SEC-020 (Emergency Station Evacuation), which mandates the immediate preparation of escape vessels and coordination with MASA’s ground stations for rescue operations.

Each of these protocols is designed to ensure crew safety while maintaining station integrity. Regular drills, conducted under MASA-OPS-015, ensure that all crew members are familiar with emergency procedures and can respond effectively under pressure. The station’s redundancy systems, particularly in Jaguar, provide additional layers of protection, allowing for continued operations even in the event of partial system failures.
