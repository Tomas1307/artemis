# MASA-DOC-027: Mission Report: Cóndor-5 — Automated Cargo Resupply

## Mission Summary

The Cóndor-5 mission, designated as an automated cargo resupply operation, commenced on 2030-11-22 and concluded successfully on 2030-11-28, spanning a total of six days. The primary objective was to receive an automated supply vessel at the Vicuña docking port and transfer 2.4 tonnes of supplies to Kuntur Station. The mission was executed by a two-person team consisting of Pilot Ana Valdivia and Engineer Pavel Kozlov, both of whom played critical roles in ensuring the successful completion of the operation. The mission was marked by a seamless docking procedure on the first attempt, a minor pressure differential of 3.2 kPa that was promptly resolved, and the efficient transfer of all cargo without incident. This mission underscores the operational maturity of MASA’s automated docking systems and the effectiveness of the crew’s training under MASA-OPS-003 (Docking and Undocking Procedure).

The success of Cóndor-5 is particularly notable given the critical nature of cargo resupply missions in maintaining the station’s operational continuity. The 2.4 tonnes of supplies included essential consumables such as food, water, and scientific equipment, all of which are vital for sustaining the crew and ongoing research activities aboard Kuntur Station. The mission’s outcome reinforces the reliability of the Vicuña docking port, which has been designed to handle automated cargo vessels with minimal crew intervention. The minor pressure differential of 3.2 kPa, while within acceptable limits, triggered MASA-SEC-013 (Docking Bay Pressure Anomaly), a high-severity protocol that ensures immediate corrective action to prevent any potential compromise to the station’s structural integrity or crew safety.

## Docking Sequence and Pressure Differential (3.2 kPa)

The docking sequence for Cóndor-5 was initiated on 2030-11-22, with the automated supply vessel approaching the Vicuña docking port under the supervision of Pilot Ana Valdivia. The vessel’s approach was monitored in real-time from the Colibrí module, where Valdivia coordinated with Engineer Pavel Kozlov, who was stationed at the Vicuña docking control panel. The docking procedure adhered strictly to MASA-OPS-003 (Docking and Undocking Procedure), which outlines the step-by-step protocols for aligning, capturing, and securing automated vessels. The first attempt at docking was successful, a testament to the precision of the vessel’s navigation systems and the crew’s adherence to procedural guidelines.

Upon initial contact, telemetry data indicated a pressure differential of 3.2 kPa between the supply vessel and the Vicuña docking port. While this differential was within the acceptable range for docking operations, it necessitated immediate attention to ensure that the pressure equalization process proceeded without risk to the station’s internal environment. The pressure differential of 3.2 kPa is significant because it approaches the threshold where minor structural stresses could occur if not addressed promptly. In response, MASA-SEC-013 (Docking Bay Pressure Anomaly) was activated, classifying the situation as a high-severity alert with a module-only scope. This protocol mandates the isolation of the docking port from the rest of the station until pressure equalization is confirmed.

Engineer Kozlov initiated the pressure equalization sequence, which involved gradually adjusting the pressure within the docking port to match that of the supply vessel. The process was closely monitored to ensure that the differential did not exceed the critical threshold that could trigger structural integrity concerns. Once the pressure was stabilized, the docking port’s seals were verified for integrity, and the vessel was securely latched to the station. The successful resolution of the pressure differential underscores the effectiveness of the station’s automated systems and the crew’s ability to respond to anomalies in real-time.

## Cargo Manifest (2.4 tonnes)

The cargo manifest for Cóndor-5 included a total of 2.4 tonnes of supplies, which were meticulously inventoried and transferred to the station over the course of the mission. The manifest was divided into three primary categories: consumables, scientific equipment, and maintenance supplies. The consumables, which accounted for approximately 1.2 tonnes of the total cargo, included food rations, water, and medical supplies. These items are critical for sustaining the crew’s daily needs and ensuring that the station’s life support systems remain operational. The scientific equipment, weighing in at 0.8 tonnes, included new experiment modules for ongoing research in the Quetzal module, where specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan are conducting studies on microgravity’s effects on biological systems. The remaining 0.4 tonnes consisted of maintenance supplies, including spare parts for the station’s power and life support systems, which are essential for preventive maintenance under MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance).

The transfer of the cargo was conducted in accordance with MASA-OPS-005 (Cargo Transfer and Inventory Update), which outlines the procedures for safely moving supplies from the docking port to their designated storage locations within the station. Pilot Valdivia oversaw the transfer process, ensuring that each item was logged into the station’s inventory management system. Engineer Kozlov, meanwhile, conducted inspections of the cargo to verify that no damage had occurred during transit. The transfer process was completed without incident, and all 2.4 tonnes of supplies were successfully integrated into the station’s inventory.

## Transfer Completion Report

The transfer completion report for Cóndor-5 was finalized on 2030-11-28, marking the official conclusion of the mission. The report confirmed that all 2.4 tonnes of cargo had been successfully transferred and inventoried, with no discrepancies noted between the manifest and the actual supplies received. The report also highlighted the successful resolution of the pressure differential of 3.2 kPa, which had been addressed without any impact on the station’s operations or the crew’s safety. The report was submitted to Commander Santiago Reyes in the Cóndor module, who reviewed the findings and approved the mission’s closure.

The success of Cóndor-5 is a testament to the effectiveness of MASA’s operational protocols and the proficiency of the crew in executing complex missions. The seamless docking procedure, the prompt resolution of the pressure differential, and the efficient transfer of cargo all contribute to the mission’s classification as a success. The lessons learned from this mission will be incorporated into future operations, particularly in refining the procedures for handling pressure differentials during docking operations. The crew’s performance under MASA-OPS-003 and MASA-SEC-013 demonstrates the importance of adherence to protocols and the value of real-time monitoring in ensuring mission success.

In conclusion, Cóndor-5 represents a significant milestone in MASA’s ongoing efforts to maintain the operational readiness of Kuntur Station. The mission’s success ensures that the station remains well-supplied and capable of supporting its crew and scientific objectives for the foreseeable future. The collaboration between Pilot Ana Valdivia and Engineer Pavel Kozlov, along with the support of the entire crew, exemplifies the teamwork and dedication that are the hallmarks of MASA’s operations.
