# MASA-DOC-014: Power Distribution and Management Guide

## Station Power Architecture

The Kuntur Station power architecture is designed around a dual-bus, redundant distribution system that ensures continuous operation even in the event of a single-point failure. Primary power is generated by two independent solar panel arrays, each capable of producing up to peak power, with energy stored in a lithium-ion battery bank consisting of 240 cells arranged in 12 parallel strings of 20 series-connected cells. This configuration provides a nominal storage capacity of energy, sufficient to maintain essential systems for a duration during eclipse periods. The distribution system operates at a voltage, with step-down converters providing lower voltages for subsystem requirements. Power management is centralized in the Condor module, where the Main Power Control Unit (MPCU) regulates distribution across all six modules via six dedicated Power Distribution Units (PDUs), each equipped with solid-state circuit breakers capable of interrupting fault currents within a duration.

## Solar Panel Array and Battery Bank

The solar panel arrays are deployed in a dual-wing configuration, each wing consisting of multiple photovoltaic panels arranged in 10 parallel strings of 16 series-connected panels. Maximum power point tracking (MPPT) is implemented at the string level, allowing each of the 20 MPPT controllers to optimize power extraction independently. The arrays are mounted on a two-axis gimbal system that maintains solar pointing accuracy within a tolerance, ensuring optimal energy capture during the station's orbital period. The battery management system (BMS) employs a distributed architecture with one BMS controller per 20-cell string, providing cell-level monitoring of voltage, temperature, and state of charge. During eclipse periods, the BMS automatically transitions to discharge mode, with the system designed to maintain battery state of charge above a level to prevent deep discharge conditions that could reduce battery lifespan.

## Per-Module Power Allocation and Rated Capacity

Each of the six modules—Condor, Quetzal, Jaguar, Colibri, Vicuna, and Tucan—receives power through dedicated PDUs with identical rated capacities of power. Under normal operating conditions, module power consumption should not exceed 80% of rated capacity, which corresponds to a portion of the module's power capacity. This threshold is established to maintain operational headroom for transient loads and to prevent sustained operation near maximum capacity, which could lead to overheating of power distribution components. When power consumption in any module exceeds 88% of rated capacity, a low-severity alert is generated, indicating that the module is approaching its operational limits. At 93% of rated capacity, the alert escalates to medium severity, requiring immediate attention from the duty engineer to identify and shed non-essential loads. The high-severity threshold is set at 97% of rated capacity, at which point automatic load shedding begins to prevent system overload. Any power consumption exceeding 97% of rated capacity is classified as critical, triggering MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) with station-wide scope, as this condition indicates a potential cascading failure that could affect the entire station.

## Load Priority Classification (Essential vs Non-Essential)

Power distribution on Kuntur Station is governed by a strict priority classification system that ensures critical systems remain operational even during power emergencies. Essential loads, which include life support systems, communication arrays, and navigation computers, are assigned Priority 1 status and are guaranteed power under all conditions. Priority 2 loads encompass scientific instruments, module lighting, and non-critical communication systems, which may be shed during high-severity power alerts. Priority 3 loads, including crew comfort systems such as galley equipment and personal device charging stations, are the first to be shed during medium-severity alerts. The load shedding sequence is automated through the MPCU, which follows a predefined hierarchy to maintain station safety and operational integrity. During MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing), the duty engineer reviews and updates the load priority assignments to reflect current mission requirements and crew activities.

## Power Alert Thresholds and Load Shedding Procedures

The power management system employs a tiered alert structure to provide early warning of potential overload conditions. When module power consumption exceeds 88% of rated capacity, a low-severity alert is generated, notifying the duty engineer to investigate the cause of the increased load. This threshold is set to provide early warning while allowing for temporary spikes in power demand, such as during experiment execution or cargo transfer operations. If power consumption continues to rise and exceeds 93% of rated capacity, the alert escalates to medium severity, and the MPCU begins shedding Priority 3 loads automatically. The duty engineer must then identify and manually shed additional non-essential loads to bring consumption back below the medium-severity threshold. At 97% of rated capacity, the alert escalates to high severity, and the MPCU initiates automatic shedding of Priority 2 loads. If power consumption exceeds 97% of rated capacity, the condition is classified as critical, and MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) is activated. This protocol triggers a station-wide emergency response, including the isolation of non-critical modules and the activation of backup power systems to maintain essential functions.

## Backup Power Systems

Kuntur Station is equipped with a redundant backup power system designed to maintain essential operations in the event of a primary power failure. The backup system consists of two independent fuel cell stacks, each capable of providing up to continuous power. The fuel cells are supplied by hydrogen and oxygen stored in high-pressure tanks located in the Jaguar module, with sufficient fuel to maintain essential systems for a duration. In the event of a primary power failure, the backup system activates automatically, with the MPCU initiating a seamless transfer to backup power within a duration. The backup power system is designed to support only Priority 1 loads, ensuring that life support, communication, and navigation systems remain operational. During backup power operation, all non-essential systems are shed, and the crew is required to follow MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing) to minimize power consumption and extend the duration of backup power availability.

The backup power system is tested weekly as part of MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), with the duty engineer verifying the automatic transfer sequence and confirming that all essential systems remain operational during the transition. The fuel cell stacks are also inspected monthly to ensure proper operation and to identify any potential issues that could affect their performance during an emergency. In the event of a prolonged power outage, the crew is trained to implement manual load shedding procedures to further extend the duration of backup power availability, with the ultimate goal of maintaining station operations until primary power can be restored or until an emergency evacuation can be executed if necessary.
