# MASA-DOC-001: Cóndor Module — Technical Operations Manual

## Module Overview and Primary Function

The Cóndor module serves as the command and control hub for all station operations aboard Kuntur Station, operating in Low Earth Orbit at a high altitude. Designed to accommodate two crew members—specifically the station commander and pilot—Cóndor integrates critical systems including ventilation, heating, lighting, cooling, and filtration to maintain optimal environmental conditions for continuous operational readiness. As the central nervous system of the station, Cóndor processes telemetry from all connected modules, executes orbital maneuvers, and coordinates communication windows with MASA ground stations. The module's primary function extends beyond mere environmental control; it houses the main flight control workstations, navigation systems, and station-wide monitoring interfaces that enable real-time decision-making for both routine operations and emergency scenarios.

The architectural design of Cóndor prioritizes redundancy in command pathways, with triple-modular redundancy in critical flight systems to ensure uninterrupted control even during partial system failures. This redundancy architecture directly supports the module's role as the primary interface for orbital adjustment maneuvers (MASA-OPS-007) and communication window management (MASA-OPS-008). The module's environmental systems are calibrated to maintain precise conditions that support both human occupancy and the sensitive electronics required for command operations, with thermal management being particularly critical given the heat generated by continuous computing loads.

## Command and Control Systems Architecture

The command architecture within Cóndor employs a distributed processing model with three primary control nodes: Navigation Control (NAV-CON), Station Management (STM-MGT), and Environmental Control (ENV-CON). NAV-CON handles orbital mechanics calculations, attitude control, and propulsion system interfaces, operating with a processing latency of less than a short duration to ensure responsive maneuver execution. STM-MGT coordinates module interconnections, power distribution, and crew scheduling, while ENV-CON regulates the five environmental parameters critical to crew safety and equipment function.

Each control node features hot-swappable processing units that can be replaced during operations without system downtime, a capability tested during MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance) procedures. The architecture incorporates a dedicated emergency control bus that activates automatically when primary systems detect faults, ensuring command continuity even during catastrophic failures. This emergency bus has direct hardware links to MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) and MASA-SEC-007 (Communication Blackout Protocol) activation circuits, allowing immediate protocol execution without software mediation.

## Telemetry Specifications and Operational Ranges

### Temperature Management

Under normal operating conditions, Cóndor maintains an internal temperature range between 20.0°C and 24.0°C, optimized for both crew comfort and equipment performance. The cooling system activates when temperatures approach 24.0°C, with redundant heat exchangers capable of removing a significant thermal load. Temperatures exceeding 30.0°C trigger a low-severity alert, typically indicating partial cooling loop obstruction or increased solar loading. At 40.0°C, the condition escalates to medium severity, requiring manual inspection of the cooling system's ammonia loops. The critical threshold of 55.0°C represents the thermal limit for the module's avionics systems; beyond this point, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) activates automatically, with the module's halogen-based fire suppression system deploying within seconds.

### Pressure Regulation

Cóndor's atmospheric pressure is maintained between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa, mirroring sea-level conditions to minimize physiological stress on crew members. Pressure dropping below 95.0 kPa initiates a low-severity alert, often caused by minor leaks in the module's sealing systems. At 90.0 kPa, the alert becomes medium severity, prompting activation of the module's backup oxygen tanks to compensate for the pressure drop. The critical threshold of 85.0 kPa triggers MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response), which includes automatic sealing of the module's hatch and activation of the station-wide pressure equalization system. This protocol's critical severity reflects the immediate danger to crew members from hypoxia and ebullism at pressures below this threshold.

### Oxygen Concentration

Normal oxygen levels in Cóndor range from 19.5% to 23.5%, balancing crew respiratory needs with fire safety considerations. Oxygen concentrations falling below 18.0% generate a low-severity alert, typically resolved by adjusting the oxygen generation system's output. At 16.0%, the alert escalates to medium severity, requiring immediate corrective action through MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance). The critical threshold of 14.0% activates MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), which includes automatic module evacuation and activation of emergency oxygen masks. This threshold was established based on the minimum oxygen concentration required to maintain crew consciousness and cognitive function during emergency response procedures.

### Radiation Monitoring

Cóndor's radiation shielding maintains normal exposure levels below 0.5 mSv/hour during standard orbital conditions. Readings between 0.5 mSv/hour and 1.0 mSv/hour trigger low-severity alerts, often associated with minor solar activity. At 2.0 mSv/hour, the alert becomes medium severity, prompting activation of additional shielding and crew relocation to more protected areas of the module. The critical threshold of 5.0 mSv/hour activates MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol), a station-wide response that includes sealing all modules and activating the station's electromagnetic shielding. This threshold represents the maximum radiation exposure considered safe for short-term crew occupancy without immediate medical intervention.

### Humidity Control

Humidity levels in Cóndor are maintained between 40% and 60% to prevent both equipment corrosion and crew discomfort. Humidity dropping below 40% but remaining above 30% generates a low-severity alert, typically resolved by adjusting the water recovery system's output. Similar low-severity alerts occur when humidity exceeds 60% but remains below 70%. Medium-severity alerts are triggered when humidity falls below 30% or rises above 70%, requiring manual intervention in the module's dehumidification systems. The critical thresholds of 15% and 90% represent conditions that can cause immediate equipment failure or crew health issues, respectively, though these conditions rarely occur under normal operations.

### Power Distribution

Cóndor's power systems operate at up to 80% of rated capacity under normal conditions, with the remaining 20% reserved for emergency systems and redundancy. Power consumption exceeding 88% of capacity triggers a low-severity alert, indicating potential overload conditions. At 93% capacity, the alert becomes medium severity, prompting activation of MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing) to redistribute loads across the station. The critical threshold of 97% capacity activates MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure), which includes automatic shedding of non-critical loads and activation of backup power systems. This threshold was established to prevent cascading power failures that could compromise the entire station's operations.

## Assigned Crew and Duty Stations

Commander Santiago Reyes, a Colombian national with extensive experience in orbital mechanics, serves as the primary operator of Cóndor's command systems. His responsibilities include overseeing all station operations, executing orbital maneuvers, and coordinating emergency responses. Pilot Ana Valdivia, a Peruvian national assigned to the Colibrí module, serves as the secondary operator for Cóndor's systems during her assigned shifts. Her expertise in spacecraft systems and emergency procedures provides critical redundancy in command operations.

During standard operations, Commander Reyes conducts daily system health checks (MASA-OPS-001) and coordinates with other module specialists to ensure station-wide operational readiness. Pilot Valdivia assumes primary command responsibilities during Commander Reyes' off-duty periods, with a formal handover procedure (MASA-OPS-006) ensuring continuity of operations. Both crew members undergo regular training in emergency protocols, with particular emphasis on MASA-SEC-001 through MASA-SEC-004, which represent the most critical emergency scenarios for the command module.

## Interconnections with Other Modules

Cóndor maintains direct data and power connections with all other station modules, with primary interfaces to the Quetzal laboratory module and the Jaguar life support module. The connection to Quetzal enables real-time monitoring of scientific experiments and data transmission to ground stations, while the Jaguar interface provides critical life support telemetry and environmental control data. Power distribution from Cóndor to other modules is managed through a smart grid system that can isolate faulty sections without disrupting critical operations.

The module's communication systems interface with the Tucán medical module to provide real-time health monitoring data for all crew members, enabling immediate response to medical emergencies through MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response). Structural integrity data from the Vicuña module is continuously monitored by Cóndor's systems, with automatic alerts generated for any anomalies that could affect station safety. The Colibrí module, while primarily serving as crew quarters, provides backup command capabilities that can be activated in the event of Cóndor system failures.

## Maintenance Schedule and Known Issues

Preventive maintenance for Cóndor's systems follows the weekly schedule defined by MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), which covers life support filters, power cells, and cooling systems every Sunday over a four-hour maintenance window. Additional deep maintenance of the oxygen generation system, CO2 scrubbers, and water recycling components is performed bi-weekly under MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance), with each session lasting approximately three hours. Known issues include intermittent telemetry dropouts from the radiation monitoring system, which are typically resolved by rebooting the system's processing unit.

A recurring issue with the module's pressure regulation system has been observed during orbital maneuvers, where rapid attitude changes can cause temporary pressure fluctuations. While these fluctuations rarely exceed the low-severity alert threshold of 95.0 kPa, they are monitored closely during all maneuver operations. Software updates and sensor calibrations are performed monthly under MASA-OPS-014, during which the station enters a reduced operational mode for approximately four hours.

All maintenance activities are logged in the station's maintenance management system, with particular attention given to components that have demonstrated higher failure rates. The module's redundancy systems are tested monthly to ensure immediate activation during emergency scenarios, with results reported to both station command and MASA ground control for analysis and potential system improvements.
