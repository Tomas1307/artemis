# MASA-DOC-006: Tucán Module — Crew Quarters and Medical Facilities Manual

## Module Overview and Habitational Function

The Tucán module serves as the primary habitational and medical facility aboard Kuntur Station, designed to accommodate the full crew complement of six personnel in individual cabins while providing comprehensive medical capabilities for both routine healthcare and emergency response. As the only module dedicated to crew welfare, Tucán integrates ventilation, heating, lighting, cooling, and filtration systems to maintain a stable habitable environment. The module's operational parameters are tightly controlled to ensure crew comfort and safety, with temperature maintained between 20.0°C and 25.0°C under normal conditions, reflecting optimal human thermal comfort ranges while accounting for the microgravity environment's altered convective heat transfer characteristics.

Atmospheric pressure is regulated between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa, replicating sea-level conditions to minimize physiological stress on crew members during extended missions. Oxygen levels are maintained between 19.5% and 23.5%, balancing the need for adequate oxygenation with fire prevention considerations in the enclosed environment. The module's radiation shielding maintains normal exposure levels below 0.5 mSv/hour, with critical thresholds set at 5.0 mSv/hour to trigger station-wide emergency protocols. Humidity control systems operate to keep relative humidity between 40% and 60%, preventing both microbial growth at higher levels and static electricity buildup at lower levels. Power consumption is monitored as a percentage of rated capacity, with normal operations not exceeding 80% to ensure system longevity and redundancy.

## Cabin Layout (6 Individual Cabins)

The Tucán module contains six identical individual cabins, each providing ample personal space per crew member. Each cabin is equipped with a sleep restraint system, personal storage compartments with a total volume of sufficient storage, and an integrated workstation with network connectivity to station systems. The sleep restraint system incorporates adjustable tension straps to accommodate different body sizes and preferred sleeping positions in microgravity, with load-bearing capacity tested to ensure safety during station maneuvers or unexpected accelerations.

Ventilation in each cabin is maintained at appropriate airflow velocity, with individual temperature control allowing adjustments to accommodate personal preferences while remaining within the module's overall thermal management parameters. Acoustic damping materials reduce ambient noise levels to below acceptable levels during normal operations, with emergency alarms set to ensure audibility while minimizing hearing damage risk. Each cabin features redundant lighting systems with both white and red spectrum LEDs, the latter used during night cycles to preserve circadian rhythms without disrupting melatonin production.

Privacy is maintained through sound-absorbing partitions with effective sound transmission reduction, effectively blocking conversational speech while allowing emergency alarms to penetrate. The cabin doors incorporate both manual and automated operation, with emergency release mechanisms that can be activated from either side. Fire suppression systems in each cabin are tied to the module-wide MASA-SEC-003 protocol, with local sensors capable of detecting temperature rises exceeding 52.0°C, the critical threshold that triggers automatic suppression measures.

## Medical Bay Equipment and Capabilities

The Tucán medical bay occupies sufficient floor space of the module's total area, equipped with diagnostic, treatment, and surgical capabilities sufficient for handling most medical emergencies that may occur during the station's operational lifetime. Core equipment includes a full-body MRI scanner with high field strength, capable of producing high-resolution images with a thin slice thickness. The ultrasound system operates at appropriate frequencies, with Doppler capability for cardiovascular assessments. A comprehensive blood analysis system can process multiple parameters simultaneously from a small sample, with results available quickly.

Life support equipment includes two ventilators capable of delivering appropriate tidal volumes at suitable rates, with FiO2 adjustable to appropriate levels. The module maintains a pharmaceutical inventory of various medications, with refrigerated storage for temperature-sensitive compounds at appropriate temperatures. Surgical capabilities are supported by a complete sterile field setup, with instruments maintained in nitrogen-purged storage to prevent oxidation. The medical bay's environmental controls are set to more stringent parameters than the general habitable areas, with temperature maintained at appropriate levels and humidity at optimal levels to optimize both equipment performance and patient comfort.

Emergency medical response is governed by MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response), which activates when any crew member's vital signs fall outside predetermined safe ranges or when the medical officer declares a high-severity condition. The protocol includes provisions for rapid patient stabilization, module-wide alert notifications, and coordination with ground-based medical support teams. The medical bay is equipped with telemedicine capabilities, including high-definition video conferencing with appropriate bandwidth and real-time vital signs transmission with secure encryption.

## Telemetry Specifications and Operational Ranges

The Tucán module's environmental control systems are monitored through a comprehensive telemetry network that samples various parameters at frequencies ranging from appropriate intervals for slow-changing variables like atmospheric composition to appropriate intervals for critical systems such as pressure and temperature. Temperature telemetry is particularly critical, with normal operating ranges between 20.0°C and 25.0°C. Deviations beyond 25.0°C but below 30.0°C trigger low-severity alerts, typically indicating minor cooling system inefficiencies or temporary heat loads from adjacent modules. As temperatures approach 40.0°C, medium-severity alerts activate, requiring immediate investigation of the cooling loop and potential activation of backup systems. The critical threshold of 52.0°C represents the point at which electronic components begin to fail and the risk of fire becomes significant, automatically triggering MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression).

Atmospheric pressure monitoring maintains even tighter control bands, with normal operations between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa. Pressure drops below 99.0 kPa but above 95.0 kPa initiate low-severity alerts, often caused by minor leaks in the module's integrity or temporary fluctuations during airlock operations. As pressure falls below 90.0 kPa, medium-severity alerts require immediate leak detection procedures and potential isolation of affected sections. The critical threshold of 85.0 kPa triggers MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response), as this pressure represents the lower limit for safe human exposure without risk of hypoxia or decompression sickness.

Oxygen concentration is maintained between 19.5% and 23.5% under normal conditions, with the lower bound set to prevent hypoxia and the upper bound to minimize fire risk. Oxygen levels dropping below 19.5% but remaining above 18.0% trigger low-severity alerts, typically indicating minor inefficiencies in the oxygen generation system. As levels fall below 16.0%, medium-severity alerts require immediate corrective action, including activation of backup oxygen supplies. The critical threshold of 14.0% triggers MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), as this concentration represents the minimum required to maintain consciousness and cognitive function.

Radiation monitoring maintains normal exposure levels below 0.5 mSv/hour, with low-severity alerts triggered between 0.5 mSv/hour and 1.0 mSv/hour. These levels typically indicate minor solar activity or temporary shielding inefficiencies. Medium-severity alerts activate between 1.0 mSv/hour and 2.0 mSv/hour, requiring crew to limit exposure time in affected areas. High-severity alerts occur between 2.0 mSv/hour and 5.0 mSv/hour, with MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory) providing guidance on exposure limitation. The critical threshold of 5.0 mSv/hour triggers MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol), requiring all crew to relocate to shielded areas of the station.

Humidity control maintains normal levels between 40% and 60% relative humidity. Deviations into the 30%-39% or 61%-70% ranges trigger low-severity alerts, indicating minor environmental control issues that could affect crew comfort or equipment performance. Medium-severity alerts activate in the 20%-29% or 71%-80% ranges, where microbial growth becomes a significant concern at high humidity and static electricity risks increase at low humidity. High-severity alerts occur in the 15%-19% or 81%-90% ranges, with critical thresholds set at below 15% and above 90%, representing conditions that could damage sensitive medical equipment or create significant health risks.

Power consumption is monitored as a percentage of rated capacity, with normal operations not exceeding 80%. Low-severity alerts trigger between 80% and 88%, indicating increased demand that may require load balancing. Medium-severity alerts activate between 88% and 93%, requiring immediate investigation of power-hungry systems. High-severity alerts occur between 93% and 97%, with MASA-SEC-018 (Power Fluctuation Response) providing guidance on non-critical system shutdowns. The critical threshold of 97% triggers MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure), as this represents the point at which system overloads and cascading failures become likely.

## Crew Welfare and Recreation Facilities

Beyond its primary habitational and medical functions, the Tucán module incorporates several features designed to maintain crew morale and psychological well-being during extended missions. The central common area features a large observation window with electrochromic glass that can be adjusted from fully transparent to opaque, providing both Earth observation opportunities and privacy as needed. This window is positioned to offer optimal views of South America during orbital passes, with the glass treated to block most ultraviolet radiation while maintaining high visible light transmission.

Recreational equipment includes a resistance exercise system capable of providing substantial resistance through a flywheel mechanism, allowing for both aerobic and strength training exercises. The system is equipped with vibration isolation mounts to prevent transmission of exercise-induced vibrations to sensitive medical equipment. A virtual reality entertainment system provides access to a library of extensive content, including films, documentaries, and interactive experiences, with the capability to support multiplayer gaming sessions between crew members.

The module's galley area is equipped with a water dispenser capable of delivering both ambient temperature and heated water, with an appropriate flow rate. Food preparation surfaces incorporate magnetic restraints to secure utensils and containers during use. The dining area features a table with six individual restraint points, allowing the full crew to gather for meals while maintaining the option for individual workspaces during off-meal periods.

Acoustic environment control includes a white noise generation system that can produce appropriate frequencies at adjustable volumes, helping to mask equipment noise and create a more restful environment during sleep periods. The module's lighting system incorporates circadian rhythm support, with color temperature adjustable to simulate natural daylight cycles. This system is programmed to follow a 24-hour cycle synchronized with mission control's time zone, though individual crew members can adjust their cabin lighting schedules to accommodate personal preferences or experiment requirements.

## Assigned Medical Officer Responsibilities

Dr. Lucía Mendoza, the Chilean medical officer assigned to the Tucán module, bears primary responsibility for all health-related aspects of station operations. Her duties encompass daily health checks for all crew members, administration of MASA-OPS-012 (Crew Medical Examination) procedures, and maintenance of the medical bay's equipment and pharmaceutical inventory. Dr. Mendoza conducts weekly inspections of all medical systems, verifying calibration of diagnostic equipment and testing emergency response systems in accordance with MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill) protocols.

As the primary responder for any medical emergencies, Dr. Mendoza is responsible for activating MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) when conditions warrant, coordinating with both the affected crew member and station command to implement appropriate treatment protocols. She maintains direct communication with MASA's ground-based medical support team, participating in weekly telemedicine conferences to review crew health status and discuss any emerging medical concerns. Dr. Mendoza also oversees the module's environmental health, monitoring air and water quality parameters and coordinating with the station's engineering team to address any deviations from normal ranges.

In addition to her clinical responsibilities, Dr. Mendoza plays a crucial role in crew psychological support, conducting regular private consultations with each crew member and organizing group activities designed to maintain team cohesion. She maintains detailed medical records for each crew member, updating them after every examination and ensuring all data is securely transmitted to ground stations during each communication window. Dr. Mendoza also manages the module's recreational facilities, ensuring they remain operational and coordinating their use to accommodate all crew members' schedules.

The medical officer's responsibilities extend to emergency preparedness, with Dr. Mendoza conducting monthly reviews of all emergency medical equipment and participating in station-wide emergency drills. She maintains familiarity with all relevant security protocols, particularly MASA-SEC-005, MASA-SEC-019 (Biological Contamination Containment), and MASA-SEC-009 (Toxic Atmosphere Detection), ensuring rapid and appropriate response to any health-threatening situations. Dr. Mendoza also serves as the primary liaison with the station's engineering team regarding life support systems, providing medical perspective on environmental control parameters and their potential health impacts.
