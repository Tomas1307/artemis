# MASA-DOC-042: Crew Health and Fitness Program

## Medical Examination Schedule and Procedures

The health and operational readiness of the Kuntur Station crew are maintained through a rigorous medical examination schedule, with MASA-OPS-012 (Crew Medical Examination) conducted weekly by Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza in the Tucan module. Each examination lasts 60 minutes and includes a comprehensive assessment of cardiovascular health, muscular integrity, bone density, and psychological well-being. The Tucan module, equipped with advanced diagnostic systems, serves as the primary medical hub for these examinations, ensuring that all life support systems are functioning optimally before and during each session.

The weekly schedule ensures that each of the six crew members—Commander Santiago Reyes, Pilot Ana Valdivia, Specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan, Engineer Pavel Kozlov, and Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza—receives individualized attention. Given the diverse specializations and physical demands of their roles, these examinations are tailored to address module-specific risks. For instance, Engineer Pavel Kozlov, responsible for mechanical systems in the Jaguar module, undergoes additional musculoskeletal evaluations due to the physical demands of maintenance tasks, while Pilot Ana Valdivia’s examinations emphasize vestibular and ocular health to support orbital navigation duties in the Colibri module.

In the event of a medical emergency, MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) is activated, with Lucía Mendoza leading the response. This protocol ensures rapid mobilization of medical resources within the affected module, with support from the Condor command center under Commander Santiago Reyes. The Tucan module’s medical systems are integrated with station-wide life support, allowing for real-time monitoring of vital signs and environmental conditions that could impact crew health.

## Physical Fitness Requirements

Physical fitness is a critical component of crew health, particularly in the microgravity environment of Kuntur Station. Each crew member is required to engage in physical activity daily, divided into cardiovascular, resistance, and flexibility training. The Quetzal module, shared by Specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan, is equipped with advanced exercise equipment, including a treadmill with vibration isolation, a cycle ergometer, and a resistance exercise device (RED). These systems are calibrated to counteract the effects of muscle atrophy and bone density loss, which are significant risks during prolonged spaceflight.

The fitness regimen is designed to address the specific physical demands of each crew member’s role. For example, Engineer Pavel Kozlov’s routine includes additional resistance training to maintain the strength required for mechanical system repairs in the Jaguar module, while Pilot Ana Valdivia’s regimen emphasizes core stability and vestibular exercises to enhance her ability to manage orbital maneuvers in the Colibri module. Commander Santiago Reyes, with 18 years of experience across multiple missions, incorporates high-intensity interval training (HIIT) to maintain peak cardiovascular health, reflecting the leadership demands of his role in the Condor module.

Regular fitness assessments are conducted as part of MASA-OPS-012, with Lucía Mendoza monitoring progress and adjusting regimens as needed. These assessments include measurements of maximal oxygen uptake (VO2 max), muscular strength, and flexibility, all of which are critical for maintaining operational readiness. Should a crew member exhibit signs of physical degradation, such as reduced muscle mass or decreased cardiovascular efficiency, corrective measures are implemented immediately to prevent long-term health issues.

## Nutrition Guidelines

Nutrition plays a vital role in sustaining crew health and performance aboard Kuntur Station. The nutrition program is designed to provide a balanced intake of macronutrients and micronutrients, with daily caloric requirements ranging from high to very high amounts per crew member, depending on individual metabolic needs and physical activity levels. The Vicuna module serves as the primary galley and food storage area, with meals prepared under the supervision of Lucía Mendoza to ensure adherence to dietary guidelines.

The diet is rich in protein to counteract muscle atrophy, with protein intake monitored per kilogram of body weight daily. Additionally, calcium and vitamin D intake is closely monitored to mitigate bone density loss, a common issue in microgravity environments. Crew members are encouraged to consume calcium and vitamin D daily, with supplements provided as necessary. Hydration is equally critical, with water intake monitored per day to prevent dehydration and support metabolic functions.

Specialized meal plans are tailored to the unique demands of each crew member’s role. For instance, Engineer Pavel Kozlov’s diet includes additional calories and protein to support the physical demands of his maintenance tasks in the Jaguar module, while Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan’s meals are enriched with antioxidants to support her biological and environmental research in the Quetzal module. The nutrition program is reviewed weekly as part of MASA-OPS-012, with adjustments made based on individual health assessments and feedback from the crew.

## Sleep Hygiene and Circadian Rhythm Management

Maintaining healthy sleep patterns is essential for cognitive function and overall well-being aboard Kuntur Station. The station operates on a 24-hour circadian cycle synchronized with Coordinated Universal Time (UTC), with crew members adhering to an 8-hour sleep schedule. Each module is equipped with individualized sleep quarters designed to minimize noise and light disruption, with ambient conditions maintained at comfortable levels to optimize comfort.

The Condor module, under the oversight of Commander Santiago Reyes, serves as the central hub for circadian rhythm management, with lighting systems programmed to simulate natural daylight cycles. Blue-enriched light is used during the day to promote alertness, while red-enriched light in the evening facilitates melatonin production to support sleep. Crew members are encouraged to follow a consistent pre-sleep routine, including relaxation exercises and the avoidance of stimulants such as caffeine in the hours leading up to sleep.

Sleep quality is monitored continuously through wearable sensors that track heart rate, respiratory rate, and movement. Data is transmitted to the Tucan module, where Lucía Mendoza reviews it as part of MASA-OPS-012. Should a crew member exhibit signs of sleep deprivation or circadian misalignment, corrective measures are implemented, including adjustments to lighting schedules, the introduction of sleep aids, or temporary reductions in workload. These interventions are critical for preventing cognitive impairment and ensuring operational readiness, particularly during high-stakes activities such as docking maneuvers or emergency response scenarios.

## Psychological Support Resources

The psychological well-being of the crew is a priority for MASA, given the isolated and confined environment of Kuntur Station. Psychological support is integrated into the weekly medical examinations conducted by Lucía Mendoza, with crew members encouraged to discuss any stressors or concerns in a confidential setting. The Tucan module is equipped with private counseling spaces, allowing for one-on-one sessions as needed.

In addition to individual support, group debriefing sessions are held bi-weekly, facilitated by Commander Santiago Reyes. These sessions provide an opportunity for crew members to share experiences, discuss challenges, and reinforce team cohesion. The Condor module’s command center serves as the primary venue for these sessions, with participation from all six crew members. The sessions are structured to address both mission-related stressors and personal concerns, ensuring that psychological health is maintained throughout the mission.

For more acute psychological issues, MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) can be activated, with Lucía Mendoza leading the response. This protocol includes provisions for psychological first aid, with support from ground-based mental health professionals via secure communication channels. The station’s communication systems, managed by Pilot Ana Valdivia in the Colibri module, ensure that crew members have regular access to private conversations with family and friends, further supporting psychological resilience.

The psychological support program is designed to be proactive, with regular assessments and interventions tailored to the unique needs of each crew member. For example, Specialist Kai Nakamura, with a background in astrophysics, may require additional support during periods of intense data analysis, while Engineer Pavel Kozlov may benefit from stress management techniques to cope with the physical demands of his role. By integrating psychological support into the broader health and fitness program, MASA ensures that the crew remains mentally and emotionally prepared for the challenges of long-duration spaceflight.
