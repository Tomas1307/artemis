# MASA-DOC-039: Inventory Management and Supply Chain Guide

## Supply Categories and Storage Locations

The Vicuña module serves as the primary cargo bay and storage facility for Kuntur Station, with an external docking port facilitating resupply missions from Earth. All incoming cargo is processed through Vicuña, where it is categorized, logged, and distributed to secondary storage locations across the station. The module maintains a normal operating temperature range of 10.0°C to 35.0°C, which is critical for preserving temperature-sensitive supplies such as medical provisions, scientific samples, and certain food items. Temperatures exceeding 45.0°C trigger a low-severity alert, indicating potential thermal stress on stored materials, while readings above 65.0°C constitute a critical emergency, risking spoilage or combustion of volatile items. In such cases, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) is activated, requiring immediate evacuation of the module.

Vicuña is divided into six primary supply categories, each with designated storage zones to optimize accessibility and environmental control. **Consumables**, including food, water, and oxygen, are stored in the forward section, where temperature and humidity are maintained within strict limits. The normal humidity range of 40% to 60% is essential for preventing desiccation or mold growth in food supplies. Humidity levels dropping below 30% or rising above 70% trigger low-severity alerts, prompting adjustments to the module’s environmental control systems. **Medical supplies**, housed in a dedicated, climate-controlled locker, require the most stringent environmental parameters, with temperature deviations beyond 35.0°C or humidity outside the 40-60% range necessitating immediate corrective action to prevent degradation of pharmaceuticals and sterile equipment.

**Scientific equipment and experiment materials** occupy the mid-section of Vicuña, where vibration damping and secure stowage are prioritized to protect sensitive instruments. This area is also equipped with additional radiation shielding, as prolonged exposure to levels exceeding 0.5 mSv/hour can compromise electronic components and experimental integrity. Radiation readings above 5.0 mSv/hour trigger MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol), a station-wide critical response that includes sealing Vicuña and relocating crew to shielded modules such as Jaguar or Condor. **Spare parts and maintenance tools** are stored in the aft section, organized by system compatibility to streamline retrieval during MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance) or unscheduled repairs. The module’s pressure is maintained between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa under normal conditions, with drops below 95.0 kPa initiating a low-severity alert. Should pressure fall below 85.0 kPa, MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) is triggered, requiring immediate isolation of Vicuña to prevent station-wide depressurization.

**EVA gear and emergency equipment**, including spacesuits, oxygen tanks, and fire suppression tools, are stored in a pressurized locker adjacent to the docking port. This location ensures rapid access during MASA-OPS-004 (EVA Preparation and Execution) or emergency scenarios. The locker is equipped with redundant power supplies, as Vicuña’s normal power consumption must not exceed 80% of rated capacity. Power levels approaching 88% trigger a low-severity alert, while readings above 97% indicate a critical overload, activating MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure). Finally, **waste and recyclables** are temporarily stored in a sealed compartment before transfer to the Colibrí module for processing. This compartment is monitored for oxygen levels, which must remain between 19.5% and 23.5% to prevent combustion risks. Oxygen concentrations below 18.0% trigger a low-severity alert, while levels dropping below 14.0% activate MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), necessitating immediate evacuation and ventilation of the compartment.

## Inventory Tracking System

The inventory tracking system aboard Kuntur Station is a centralized digital platform integrated with Vicuña’s environmental and telemetry systems. Each supply item is assigned a unique RFID tag, which is scanned upon arrival, storage, and retrieval. The system cross-references these scans with Vicuña’s environmental data to ensure that items are stored under optimal conditions. For example, if a batch of temperature-sensitive medical supplies is logged into a storage zone where the temperature exceeds 35.0°C, the system automatically flags the discrepancy and alerts the duty specialist. This integration is critical for maintaining the integrity of supplies, particularly in a module where environmental parameters can fluctuate due to docking operations or thermal loads from adjacent modules.

The tracking system is managed primarily by Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan, who oversees monthly audits as part of MASA-OPS-013 (Supply Request and Inventory Management). During these audits, Al-Hassan verifies the physical inventory against digital records, checking for discrepancies that could indicate misplacement, spoilage, or unauthorized usage. The system also tracks usage rates for consumables, projecting depletion timelines based on crew consumption patterns. For instance, oxygen usage is monitored in real time, with the system alerting the crew if consumption rates suggest reserves will drop below the 30% threshold before the next resupply mission. This proactive monitoring is essential for preventing critical shortages, as oxygen levels below 14.0% trigger MASA-SEC-002, a scenario that must be avoided at all costs.

The tracking system is backed up daily to both the Condor command module and MASA ground control, ensuring redundancy in case of a system failure in Vicuña. Power fluctuations in Vicuña, which can occur during docking operations, are closely monitored, with the system designed to withstand brief interruptions. However, sustained power levels above 93% of rated capacity trigger a medium-severity alert, prompting the crew to shed non-essential loads or initiate MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing). In the event of a critical power failure, the tracking system’s backup batteries provide sufficient operation time, allowing the crew to manually log inventory changes until power is restored.

## Resupply Request Procedures and Urgency Levels

Resupply requests are initiated through MASA-OPS-013, a procedure conducted monthly or whenever inventory reserves fall below 30% of their projected capacity. The process is led by Commander Santiago Reyes and Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan, who collaborate to prioritize requests based on urgency levels and mission criticality. Urgency levels are categorized into three tiers: **routine**, **priority**, and **emergency**. Routine requests cover standard consumables such as food, water, and non-critical spare parts, which are projected to deplete within the next 60 days. These requests are submitted as part of the monthly audit and typically take a standard fulfillment time to fulfill, depending on launch window availability from MASA’s ground facilities.

Priority requests are triggered when reserves of critical items—such as oxygen, medical supplies, or key spare parts—fall below 30% of their capacity. For example, if oxygen reserves in Vicuña drop to 29% of their total capacity, a priority request is automatically generated by the inventory tracking system. Priority requests are expedited, with MASA ground control aiming to include them in the next available resupply mission, usually within an expedited fulfillment time. The crew must also implement conservation measures, such as reducing non-essential oxygen usage or postponing experiments that require significant oxygen consumption. During this period, oxygen levels are monitored continuously, with readings below 18.0% triggering a low-severity alert and prompting manual inspections of the life support systems.

Emergency requests are reserved for scenarios where critical supplies are depleted or compromised, posing an immediate risk to crew safety or mission continuity. These requests are initiated manually by the Commander or Specialist Al-Hassan and require immediate action from MASA ground control. Examples of emergency scenarios include:
- Oxygen reserves dropping below 15%, approaching the 14.0% critical threshold that triggers MASA-SEC-002.
- A failure in the water recycling system (MASA-SEC-015) that depletes potable water reserves.
- Radiation shielding damage in Vicuña causing exposure levels to exceed 2.0 mSv/hour, necessitating replacement materials to prevent activation of MASA-SEC-004.

Emergency resupply missions are prioritized above all other launches, with MASA aiming to dispatch a cargo vessel within a rapid dispatch time of the request. In the interim, the crew must rely on redundant systems and contingency protocols to mitigate risks. For instance, if oxygen levels are critically low, the crew may isolate non-essential modules and consolidate operations in Condor or Jaguar, where life support systems are more robust.

## Minimum Reserve Thresholds

Minimum reserve thresholds are established to ensure that Kuntur Station can sustain operations for at least 30 days without resupply, accounting for potential delays in launch windows or docking complications. These thresholds are dynamically adjusted based on crew size, mission phase, and environmental conditions. For consumables, the minimum reserve threshold is set at 30% of total capacity, with the following specific benchmarks:

- **Oxygen**: The station maintains a total oxygen reserve capable of supporting the six crew members for 45 days under normal conditions. The minimum threshold is set at 30% of this capacity, equivalent to approximately a sufficient supply duration. Oxygen levels are monitored continuously, with the inventory system triggering a priority resupply request if reserves fall below this threshold. Should oxygen levels drop below 18.0%, a low-severity alert is issued, prompting the crew to inspect for leaks or inefficiencies in the life support system. Levels below 16.0% escalate to a medium-severity alert, requiring immediate corrective action, while the critical threshold of 14.0% activates MASA-SEC-002, mandating evacuation of the affected module.

- **Water**: Potable water reserves are maintained at a minimum of 30% of the station’s total capacity, which equates to roughly a sufficient daily allocation per crew member for 30 days. The water recycling system in Colibrí is designed to supplement these reserves, but any failure in this system (triggering MASA-SEC-015) necessitates strict rationing. Water reserves are monitored for both quantity and quality, with humidity levels in storage areas kept between 40% and 60% to prevent bacterial growth. Humidity deviations into the low-alert bands (30-39% or 61-70%) prompt adjustments to the environmental control systems to preserve water integrity.

- **Food**: Food reserves are stocked to provide each crew member with a sufficient caloric intake per day for 45 days, with the minimum threshold set at 30% of this total. Food items are categorized by shelf life, with perishable items prioritized for consumption before their expiration dates. Temperature control is critical for food storage, with Vicuña’s normal range of 10.0°C to 35.0°C ensuring optimal preservation. Temperatures exceeding 45.0°C risk spoilage, particularly for items stored in the forward consumables section, while temperatures below 10.0°C can cause freezing and texture degradation in certain foodstuffs.

- **Medical Supplies**: Medical reserves are maintained at a minimum of a sufficient reserve level due to the unpredictable nature of medical emergencies. This threshold includes pharmaceuticals, sterile equipment, and diagnostic tools. The medical locker in Vicuña is equipped with redundant environmental controls, as temperature and humidity deviations can render medical supplies ineffective. For example, humidity levels above 70% can compromise the sterility of surgical tools, while temperatures exceeding 35.0°C may degrade the efficacy of certain medications. In the event of a medical emergency, MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) may be activated, requiring immediate access to these reserves.

- **Spare Parts and Tools**: Spare parts are stocked based on the mean time between failures (MTBF) for critical systems. The minimum reserve threshold is set at 30% of the projected annual consumption, ensuring that the crew can perform essential repairs without awaiting resupply. For example, spare CO2 scrubber components are maintained at higher levels due to their critical role in life support, with a minimum threshold of a sufficient reserve level. A failure in the CO2 scrubbing system triggers MASA-SEC-014 (CO2 Scrubber Failure Protocol), a high-severity, station-wide response that requires immediate replacement of faulty components.

## Cargo Transfer Safety Requirements

Cargo transfer operations are governed by MASA-OPS-005 (Cargo Transfer and Inventory Update), a procedure designed to ensure the safe and efficient movement of supplies between the docked resupply vessel and Vicuña. The process begins with a pre-docking inspection of Vicuña’s environmental systems, verifying that temperature, pressure, humidity, and radiation levels are within normal ranges. Temperature must be stabilized between 10.0°C and 35.0°C, pressure between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa, and humidity between 40% and 60%. Any deviations outside these ranges must be corrected before docking is approved, as fluctuations during cargo transfer can compromise the integrity of incoming supplies.

Once the resupply vessel is securely docked, the crew conducts a pressure equalization check to ensure no differential exceeds a safe threshold between the vessel and Vicuña. A differential greater than the safe threshold can cause rapid air movement during hatch opening, potentially dislodging loosely secured cargo or creating hazardous conditions for the crew. The hatch is opened only after Commander Reyes or Specialist Al-Hassan confirms that pressure levels are stable and within the normal range. During transfer, the crew wears protective gear, including gloves and eye protection, to prevent contamination of supplies or injury from sharp edges on cargo containers.

Cargo is transferred in staged batches, with priority given to temperature-sensitive or hazardous materials. Medical supplies and scientific samples are moved first, followed by consumables and spare parts. Each item is scanned using the RFID system upon entry into Vicuña, with its storage location verified against the inventory database. The crew must also visually inspect cargo for signs of damage or contamination, particularly for items exposed to radiation during transit. Radiation levels in Vicuña are monitored continuously during transfer, with readings exceeding 1.0 mSv/hour triggering a low-severity alert. If levels approach 2.0 mSv/hour, the transfer is paused, and the crew retreats to a shielded area while the source of the radiation is identified and mitigated.

Once all cargo is transferred, the crew conducts a post-transfer inspection of Vicuña’s environmental systems to ensure no anomalies were introduced during the operation. Particular attention is paid to humidity levels, as the influx of new supplies can temporarily elevate moisture levels in the module. Humidity readings above 70% trigger a low-severity alert, prompting the crew to activate additional dehumidifiers until levels return to the normal range. The final step in MASA-OPS-005 is the updating of the inventory tracking system, which is cross-checked by both Specialist Al-Hassan and Commander Reyes to ensure accuracy. Any discrepancies are investigated immediately, with physical counts conducted if necessary to resolve inconsistencies.

In the event of a critical failure during cargo transfer—such as a sudden pressure drop below 85.0 kPa or a fire—MASA-SEC-001 or MASA-SEC-003 is activated, respectively. The crew is trained to respond to these emergencies by securing the hatch between the resupply vessel and Vicuña, isolating the module if necessary, and following the appropriate evacuation protocols. Cargo transfer operations are halted until the module is deemed safe for re-entry, and any compromised supplies are quarantined for further inspection or disposal.
