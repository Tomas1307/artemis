# MASA-DOC-026: Mission Report: Cóndor-4 — Emergency Thermal Repair

## Mission Summary and Emergency Timeline

The Cóndor-4 mission, designated as an emergency repair operation, commenced on 2030-06-05 and concluded on 2030-06-19, spanning a total of 14 days. The primary objective was the emergency replacement of the Jaguar module's thermal control unit following a critical cooling system failure. The mission was executed by Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, with Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza providing support. The urgency of the mission was underscored by the Jaguar module's temperature reaching a peak of 47.3 C, which triggered a high-severity alert. This temperature reading was dangerously close to the critical threshold, which would have activated MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression). The mission was successfully completed, restoring the Jaguar module to nominal operating conditions.

The emergency timeline began on 2030-06-05 when the initial signs of thermal instability were detected during a routine system health check as part of MASA-OPS-001. The temperature in the Jaguar module began to rise steadily, reaching a low-severity deviation. This initial increase was attributed to a transient thermal load from adjacent modules. However, the temperature continued to rise, reaching a medium-severity alert. At this point, Engineer Kozlov initiated a detailed inspection of the cooling loop, but the temperature continued to climb, reaching the high-severity threshold of 47.3 C. This critical situation necessitated immediate action, leading to the activation of the emergency repair mission.

## Jaguar Thermal Failure Analysis

The thermal failure in the Jaguar module was traced back to a malfunction in the primary cooling unit. The Jaguar module, which houses critical life support equipment, has a more restrictive thermal threshold compared to other modules. For instance, the Quetzal module's critical temperature threshold is higher, reflecting the different operational requirements and equipment sensitivities. The Jaguar module's normal operating temperature range is within nominal parameters. Temperatures beyond this range but below a low-severity deviation are classified as low-severity deviations, typically caused by transient thermal loads from adjacent modules. However, the rapid escalation to 47.3 C indicated a more severe underlying issue.

The cooling system in the Jaguar module is designed to maintain a stable thermal environment to ensure the proper functioning of life support equipment. The failure of the primary cooling unit led to a cascade of thermal issues, including increased strain on secondary cooling systems and potential risks to the integrity of the module's structural components. The high-severity alert at 47.3 C was particularly concerning because it approached the critical threshold, which would have triggered MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression). This protocol is designed to activate automatically at critical severity, requiring immediate evacuation of the module to prevent catastrophic failure.

The analysis revealed that the primary cooling unit had experienced a mechanical failure in its heat exchange mechanism. This failure was likely due to prolonged operational stress and the accumulation of micro-debris in the cooling loops. The secondary cooling systems, while functional, were insufficient to compensate for the loss of the primary unit, leading to the rapid temperature increase. The failure analysis also highlighted the need for more frequent preventive maintenance checks, as outlined in MASA-OPS-002, to ensure the early detection of potential issues in the cooling system.

## EVA Repair Operations

The emergency repair mission required an Extravehicular Activity (EVA) to replace the faulty thermal control unit. The EVA was conducted by Engineer Pavel Kozlov, with Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza providing support from the Cóndor command center. The EVA preparation and execution followed the guidelines outlined in MASA-OPS-004, ensuring that all safety protocols were adhered to and that the necessary tools and replacement parts were available.

The EVA began with a thorough pre-EVA checklist, which included the verification of spacesuit integrity, tool functionality, and communication systems. Engineer Kozlov exited the station through the airlock, carefully maneuvering to the Jaguar module's external access panel. The faulty thermal control unit was located and removed, with the replacement unit installed and secured. The entire EVA operation was conducted under the close supervision of the Cóndor command center, with real-time telemetry data being transmitted to both the station and MASA ground stations.

The successful completion of the EVA repair operations was a testament to the rigorous training and preparation of the MASA crew. The replacement of the thermal control unit restored the Jaguar module's cooling system to full functionality, allowing the temperature to return to the normal operating range. The EVA also provided an opportunity to inspect the external components of the Jaguar module for any signs of additional wear or damage, ensuring that no further issues were present.

## Temperature Readings During Incident (47.3 C peak)

The temperature readings during the incident provided critical insights into the thermal dynamics of the Jaguar module. The initial temperature rise to a low-severity deviation was classified as a minor thermal anomaly. However, the rapid escalation to a medium-severity alert prompted immediate action from the duty engineer. The temperature continued to rise, reaching a peak of 47.3 C, which triggered a high-severity alert. This reading was dangerously close to the critical threshold, which would have activated MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression).

The temperature readings were continuously monitored and transmitted to the Cóndor command center, where Commander Santiago Reyes and Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza coordinated the response efforts. The real-time telemetry data allowed for the rapid assessment of the situation and the implementation of corrective measures. The temperature readings also highlighted the importance of maintaining a stable thermal environment in the Jaguar module, given its critical role in housing life support equipment.

The peak temperature of 47.3 C was a significant concern, as it approached the critical threshold. At this threshold, the risk of thermal runaway and potential fire becomes significantly higher, necessitating immediate evacuation of the module. The successful resolution of the incident before reaching this critical threshold underscored the effectiveness of the MASA crew's response and the importance of adhering to established protocols and procedures.

## Post-Repair Verification

Following the completion of the EVA repair operations, a comprehensive post-repair verification process was conducted to ensure the successful restoration of the Jaguar module's thermal control system. The verification process included a series of system health checks, as outlined in MASA-OPS-001, to confirm that the temperature had returned to the normal operating range. The cooling system was monitored continuously for a period of 24 hours to ensure that the replacement thermal control unit was functioning correctly and that no further thermal anomalies were present.

The post-repair verification also included a thorough inspection of the Jaguar module's internal systems to ensure that no secondary damage had occurred as a result of the thermal incident. This inspection covered the life support equipment, structural components, and electrical systems, confirming that all systems were operating within nominal parameters. The successful completion of the post-repair verification process marked the official conclusion of the Cóndor-4 mission, with the Jaguar module restored to full operational capability.

The Cóndor-4 mission served as a critical learning experience for the MASA crew, highlighting the importance of proactive maintenance and the rapid response to thermal anomalies. The successful resolution of the incident underscored the effectiveness of the MASA protocols and procedures, as well as the dedication and expertise of the crew. The lessons learned from this mission will be incorporated into future training programs and operational guidelines, ensuring the continued safety and success of the Kuntur Station.
