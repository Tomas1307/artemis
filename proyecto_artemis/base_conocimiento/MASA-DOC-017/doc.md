# MASA-DOC-017: Ventilation and Filtration Systems Guide

## Ventilation Architecture and Airflow Patterns

The Kuntur Station ventilation system is designed as a closed-loop, multi-zone architecture that maintains atmospheric integrity across all six modules while accommodating varying operational demands. Airflow is managed through a primary duct network that connects Condor (command), Quetzal (laboratory), Jaguar (engineering), Colibri (docking), Vicuña (habitation), and Tucán (medical) modules in a redundant star topology centered on Condor. Each module maintains independent ventilation control but can isolate or cross-connect airflow paths via motorized dampers controlled by the Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) in Jaguar.

Normal airflow velocity through primary ducts is maintained at a moderate rate to balance efficient gas exchange with minimal acoustic disturbance, while secondary distribution ducts within each module operate at a lower rate to ensure laminar flow patterns that prevent stagnation zones. The system completes a full atmospheric circulation in a timely manner under standard conditions, with Condor serving as the central mixing chamber where air from all modules is blended before redistribution. This architecture ensures that contamination events in one module are diluted before reaching others, providing critical time for response.

During nominal operations, the system maintains a slight positive pressure gradient from Condor outward to all other modules, with Condor at a standard pressure and peripheral modules at a slightly lower pressure. This gradient ensures that any minor leaks result in outward airflow rather than inward contamination. The pressure differential is actively monitored by the ECLSS, with deviations beyond a nominal threshold triggering automatic damper adjustments to restore balance. Should pressure differentials exceed a significant threshold, MASA-SEC-011 (Moderate Pressure Drop Response) activates at high severity, requiring immediate investigation by the duty engineer.

## HEPA and Chemical Filtration Stages

The filtration system employs a three-stage process to maintain atmospheric purity: pre-filtration, HEPA filtration, and chemical adsorption. Pre-filters with a coarse rating remove large particulate matter, including skin cells and textile fibers, protecting downstream components from fouling. These are followed by HEPA filters rated at a fine level with high efficiency, which capture microbial contaminants, fine dust, and aerosolized particles. The final stage utilizes activated carbon beds impregnated with potassium iodide to adsorb volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and trace gases that could affect crew health or equipment performance.

Each module contains redundant filtration trains, allowing one to be taken offline for maintenance while the other maintains full capacity. The Jaguar module houses the primary filtration bank for the entire station, with secondary banks located in Condor and Quetzal to provide backup capacity. Filter performance is continuously monitored through differential pressure sensors across each stage. A pressure drop exceeding a significant threshold across the HEPA stage indicates impending saturation and triggers a maintenance alert under MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance).

The chemical filtration stage is particularly critical in Quetzal, where experimental protocols may release trace chemicals. The activated carbon beds in Quetzal are sized larger than in other modules to accommodate potential laboratory emissions. Should VOC sensors detect concentrations above a safe threshold, the system automatically increases airflow through the chemical filtration stage and alerts Specialist Kai Nakamura or Fátima Al-Hassan, who are responsible for laboratory safety protocols.

## Per-Module Humidity Operating Ranges

All modules maintain identical humidity operating ranges to ensure crew comfort and equipment preservation. Normal humidity levels are maintained between 40% and 60% relative humidity (RH), which prevents both microbial growth at higher levels and static electricity buildup at lower levels. The system employs condensate recovery from the air handling units to maintain these levels, with excess moisture directed to the water recycling system.

Deviations below 40% RH but above 30% RH are classified as low-severity alerts, typically caused by temporary increases in crew activity or equipment heat loads. The system responds by increasing humidification from the water recovery loop. Should humidity fall between 20% and 29% RH, a medium-severity condition is declared, requiring manual intervention to check the humidification system. Humidity levels between 15% and 19% RH trigger a high-severity alert, as prolonged exposure can cause respiratory irritation and increase static discharge risks to sensitive electronics.

Conversely, humidity above 60% RH but below 70% RH is considered a low-severity condition, often resulting from crew exercise or cooking activities in Vicuña. The system responds by increasing dehumidification capacity. Humidity between 71% and 80% RH triggers a medium-severity alert, requiring inspection of the condensate removal system. Levels between 81% and 90% RH constitute a high-severity condition, as this range promotes microbial growth and can lead to equipment corrosion. Any humidity reading below 15% RH or above 90% RH is classified as critical, requiring immediate corrective action and potential module isolation.

The Jaguar module, under the responsibility of Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, serves as the primary monitoring point for humidity across all modules. During MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check), Kozlov verifies that all modules are maintaining proper humidity levels and that the condensate recovery system is functioning nominally. The Condor module, under Commander Santiago Reyes, maintains override authority for humidity control during station-wide emergencies.

## Filter Status Monitoring and Replacement

Filter health is continuously monitored through a combination of differential pressure sensors, particulate counters, and VOC sensors. The ECLSS logs filter performance data every 5 minutes, with trends analyzed over 24-hour periods to detect gradual degradation. Differential pressure across the HEPA stage is the primary indicator of filter loading, with normal operation showing less than a nominal pressure drop. A sustained reading between a nominal pressure drop and a significant pressure drop indicates the filter is approaching its service life and triggers a maintenance notification.

When differential pressure exceeds a significant threshold, the filter is considered saturated and requires immediate replacement. The system automatically switches to the redundant filtration train while alerting the duty engineer. Filter replacement is performed according to MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), with HEPA filters typically lasting a standard operational period under normal conditions. However, Quetzal's filters may require more frequent replacement due to laboratory activities, with Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan responsible for monitoring their condition.

The chemical filtration stage is monitored through VOC breakthrough detection. When VOC concentrations downstream of the filter exceed a minimal threshold, the carbon bed is considered spent. Chemical filters are replaced periodically or after significant contamination events, whichever comes first. All filter replacements are logged in the maintenance database and reported during MASA-OPS-006 (Crew Shift Handoff) to ensure continuity of monitoring.

## Emergency Ventilation Flush Procedures

In the event of atmospheric contamination, the ventilation system can perform an emergency flush of any module. This procedure involves isolating the affected module's ventilation from the rest of the station and rapidly exchanging its atmosphere with fresh air from the station's oxygen and nitrogen reserves. The flush cycle completes in a standard operational timeframe, replacing a standard portion of the module's atmosphere.

Emergency flush is automatically triggered when toxic gas sensors detect concentrations exceeding safe limits, activating MASA-SEC-009 (Toxic Atmosphere Detection) at high severity. The system immediately isolates the affected module's ventilation, closes all interconnecting dampers, and begins the flush sequence. Crew members in the affected module must don oxygen masks and prepare for potential evacuation to adjacent modules.

During the flush procedure, the ECLSS maintains positive pressure in the affected module to prevent contamination spread. The flush air is vented to space through dedicated overboard dump valves rather than being recirculated. After completion, atmospheric samples are taken and analyzed before the module is declared safe for re-entry. Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza is responsible for evaluating crew exposure levels and determining if medical intervention is required under MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response).

## Contamination Isolation Capabilities

The ventilation system is designed with multiple layers of contamination isolation to prevent station-wide atmospheric compromise. Each module can be completely isolated from the others through a combination of motorized dampers and manual isolation valves. The primary isolation dampers are capable of sealing rapidly upon command, with secondary manual valves providing redundant sealing capability.

In the event of a biological contamination event, MASA-SEC-019 (Biological Contamination Containment) is activated at high severity. This protocol automatically isolates the affected module's ventilation, activates HEPA filtration bypass to prevent contaminant spread, and initiates decontamination procedures. The system can maintain isolation for a standard operational duration while ground control develops a response plan.

For chemical contamination events, the system can isolate and flush individual modules as described in the emergency flush procedures. The chemical filtration capacity in each module is sufficient to handle minor releases, with station-wide filtration resources available for larger events. The ECLSS continuously monitors for cross-contamination between modules, with sensors located at each damper interface to detect any leakage.

The Condor module serves as the central control point for all contamination isolation procedures. Commander Reyes has ultimate authority to initiate station-wide isolation protocols if multiple modules are affected. During such events, communication is maintained through the station's redundant data networks, with MASA-OPS-008 (Communication Window Management) ensuring continuous contact with ground control.
