# MASA-DOC-013: Thermal Management System Guide

## System Overview

The Thermal Management System (TMS) of Kuntur Station is a critical life support subsystem responsible for maintaining stable environmental conditions across all six operational modules. Operating in Low Earth Orbit at 408 km altitude, the station experiences extreme thermal fluctuations between solar exposure and eclipse phases, requiring precise temperature regulation to ensure crew safety, equipment functionality, and scientific experiment integrity. The TMS integrates active cooling loops, passive radiators, and redundant heating elements to maintain module-specific temperature ranges while compensating for orbital thermal variations.

Each module's thermal profile is independently monitored through distributed sensor networks, with telemetry data continuously transmitted to both the Cóndor command center and MASA ground stations. The system employs a tiered alert structure that escalates from low-severity deviations to critical emergencies, with automated responses triggering at predefined thresholds. Thermal management operations fall under the primary responsibility of Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, who conducts daily telemetry reviews as part of MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check) and coordinates with module specialists during off-nominal conditions.

## Module-by-Module Temperature Operating Ranges

The Cóndor module, serving as the station's command and control hub, maintains a normal operating temperature range between 20.0°C and 24.0°C to optimize crew comfort and equipment performance. This narrow band reflects the module's high concentration of electronic systems and command interfaces, which generate consistent thermal loads. Temperatures exceeding 24.0°C but remaining below 30.0°C are classified as low-severity deviations, typically resulting from increased crew activity or temporary equipment overloads. The module's critical threshold is set at 55.0°C, at which point immediate evacuation becomes necessary to prevent equipment failure and potential fire hazards.

Quetzal, the primary research laboratory, operates within a broader normal range of 18.0°C to 26.0°C to accommodate diverse experimental requirements. This flexibility allows for simultaneous operation of cryogenic storage units and high-temperature experimental setups while maintaining overall module stability. The critical threshold of 60.0°C is higher than Cóndor's, reflecting Quetzal's more robust thermal design and the presence of specialized containment systems for experimental equipment. Research Specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan are trained to implement immediate containment measures when temperatures approach the medium-alert threshold of 45.0°C.

Jaguar, housing life support systems and crew quarters, maintains the most restrictive normal operating range of 19.0°C to 23.0°C to ensure optimal human habitability. This narrow band is critical for maintaining proper sleep cycles and physiological comfort during extended missions. The module's critical threshold of 50.0°C is lower than other modules, reflecting the sensitivity of life support equipment and the potential for rapid oxygen depletion in high-temperature scenarios. Engineer Pavel Kozlov conducts bi-hourly temperature checks in Jaguar as part of MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance), with particular attention to the medium-alert threshold of 38.0°C, which triggers automatic cooling system diagnostics.

Colibrí, the docking and logistics module, operates within the widest normal temperature range of 15.0°C to 30.0°C to accommodate the thermal stresses of docking operations and cargo transfers. This flexibility is necessary due to the module's frequent exposure to external temperature fluctuations during airlock cycling and EVA preparations. The critical threshold of 65.0°C accounts for the module's reinforced thermal shielding and the transient nature of many docking-related thermal events. Pilot Ana Valdivia monitors Colibrí's thermal status during all docking operations (MASA-OPS-003) and implements cooling protocols when temperatures exceed the medium-alert threshold of 55.0°C.

Vicuña, the station's storage and supply module, maintains a normal operating range of 10.0°C to 35.0°C to preserve the integrity of food supplies, spare parts, and scientific samples. This wide range allows for efficient power management while ensuring all stored materials remain within their specified temperature tolerances. The module's critical threshold of 65.0°C matches Colibrí's, reflecting similar thermal design considerations for storage environments. During inventory operations (MASA-OPS-005), crew members conduct manual temperature verification of sensitive storage areas, particularly when readings approach the medium-alert threshold of 55.0°C.

Tucán, the medical and crew health module, operates within a normal range of 20.0°C to 25.0°C to maintain sterile conditions and optimal performance of medical equipment. This range is slightly warmer than other crew modules to accommodate medical procedures and equipment calibration requirements. The critical threshold of 52.0°C is lower than most other modules, reflecting the sensitivity of medical supplies and diagnostic equipment to elevated temperatures. Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza conducts hourly thermal checks during medical operations (MASA-OPS-012) and implements immediate cooling measures when temperatures exceed the medium-alert threshold of 40.0°C.

## Heating and Cooling System Specifications per Module

The Cóndor module's thermal management system employs a dual-loop cooling architecture with primary and secondary coolant circuits capable of dissipating up to high thermal loads. The primary loop utilizes a water-glycol mixture circulating at a high flow rate through embedded heat exchangers, while the secondary loop employs a refrigerant-based system for high-load scenarios. Heating is provided by redundant electrical resistance elements with a combined capacity of high capacity, automatically activating when temperatures approach the lower normal limit of 20.0°C. The system's rapid response capability ensures temperature stabilization within a short time of any deviation from the normal range.

Quetzal's thermal system features a more complex configuration to handle diverse experimental loads, incorporating both active cooling loops and passive thermal storage units. The primary cooling loop operates at a high flow rate with a capacity of high capacity, supplemented by phase-change material (PCM) units that absorb excess heat during peak experimental operations. Heating is provided by a combination of electrical resistance elements (high capacity) and waste heat recovery from experimental equipment. This hybrid approach allows Quetzal to maintain its broad normal operating range while accommodating sudden thermal spikes from research activities.

Jaguar's life support thermal system prioritizes reliability and redundancy, featuring triple-redundant cooling loops each capable of handling the module's high baseline thermal load. The primary and secondary loops operate in parallel at a high flow rate, with the tertiary loop held in standby mode for emergency activation. Heating is provided by a distributed network of low-wattage resistance elements (total high capacity) strategically placed throughout crew quarters to ensure uniform temperature distribution. The system's design emphasizes gradual temperature transitions to prevent sleep disruption, with cooling rates limited to a gradual rate during night cycles.

Colibrí's thermal management system is optimized for rapid response to docking-related thermal events, featuring high-capacity cooling loops capable of high heat dissipation at a high flow rate. The system incorporates direct expansion cooling for immediate heat removal during airlock operations and a regenerative thermal storage system to handle the module's frequent temperature fluctuations. Heating is provided by a combination of electrical resistance elements (high capacity) and heat recovery from docking mechanism operations. The system's unique "thermal surge" mode automatically activates when temperatures exceed 55.0°C, diverting additional coolant flow to critical docking interfaces.

Vicuña's storage-optimized thermal system employs a zoned approach, with independent temperature control for different storage categories. The primary cooling loop operates at a high flow rate with a high capacity, supplemented by dedicated cooling circuits for cryogenic storage (low-temperature capability) and temperature-sensitive scientific samples. Heating is provided by a low-power resistance network (high capacity) designed for gradual temperature increases to prevent condensation in storage areas. The system's unique feature is its "thermal mapping" capability, which creates a real-time 3D temperature profile of the module to identify hot or cold spots during inventory operations.

Tucán's medical thermal system incorporates hospital-grade temperature control with redundant cooling loops (high capacity at a high flow rate) and medical-grade heating elements (high capacity). The system features HEPA-filtered air circulation to maintain sterile conditions while managing thermal loads. A dedicated cooling circuit serves the medical imaging equipment, maintaining precise temperature control to ensure diagnostic accuracy. The system's "sterile mode" automatically activates when temperatures exceed 40.0°C, increasing airflow while maintaining positive pressure to prevent contamination.

## Alert Severity Mapping for Temperature Deviations

Temperature deviations in the Cóndor module follow a four-tier alert structure. Readings between 24.0°C and 30.0°C trigger low-severity alerts, typically addressed through automated cooling adjustments. The medium-alert threshold of 40.0°C activates MASA-OPS-011 (Power Management and Load Balancing) to reduce equipment loads, while the high-alert threshold of 55.0°C requires immediate manual intervention. Critical conditions above 55.0°C activate MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression), with Commander Santiago Reyes coordinating module evacuation.

Quetzal's alert structure accommodates its broader operating range, with low-severity alerts triggered between 26.0°C and 35.0°C. Medium alerts at 45.0°C initiate experimental equipment shutdown sequences, while high alerts at 60.0°C trigger containment protocols. Critical conditions above 60.0°C activate MASA-SEC-003, with Research Specialists Nakamura and Al-Hassan implementing emergency experiment containment measures before evacuation.

Jaguar's restrictive temperature range results in earlier alert triggers, with low-severity conditions declared between 23.0°C and 28.0°C. Medium alerts at 38.0°C activate the module's redundant cooling loops, while high alerts at 50.0°C trigger life support system safing. Critical conditions above 50.0°C immediately activate MASA-SEC-003, with Engineer Kozlov coordinating rapid evacuation to prevent life support system failure.

Colibrí's alert structure reflects its operational flexibility, with low-severity alerts between 30.0°C and 40.0°C during normal operations. Medium alerts at 55.0°C trigger docking system safing procedures, while high alerts at 65.0°C initiate airlock cooling protocols. Critical conditions above 65.0°C activate MASA-SEC-003, with Pilot Valdivia coordinating emergency docking system shutdown and crew evacuation.

Vicuña's storage-focused alert system triggers low-severity alerts between 35.0°C and 45.0°C, initiating inventory temperature verification procedures. Medium alerts at 55.0°C activate selective cooling of sensitive storage zones, while high alerts at 65.0°C trigger full storage system safing. Critical conditions above 65.0°C activate MASA-SEC-003, with the duty officer implementing emergency inventory protection measures before evacuation.

Tucán's medical alert structure prioritizes equipment protection, with low-severity alerts between 25.0°C and 30.0°C triggering automated cooling adjustments. Medium alerts at 40.0°C activate medical equipment safing sequences, while high alerts at 52.0°C initiate sterile field protection protocols. Critical conditions above 52.0°C activate MASA-SEC-003, with Medical Officer Mendoza coordinating emergency medical equipment shutdown and patient evacuation.

## Seasonal Orbital Thermal Variations

Kuntur Station experiences significant seasonal thermal variations due to its 408 km altitude and 51.6° orbital inclination. During the June solstice period, the station experiences prolonged solar exposure with eclipse periods as short as a brief duration, resulting in external temperature fluctuations between low and high temperatures. Internal module temperatures typically rise 2-3°C above normal ranges during these periods, requiring increased cooling system activity. The thermal management system automatically adjusts coolant flow rates and activates additional radiator panels during these high-solar periods, with manual oversight required during MASA-OPS-007 (Orbital Adjustment Maneuver) to prevent overheating of maneuvering thrusters.

The December solstice period presents the opposite challenge, with eclipse periods extending to a longer duration and reduced solar exposure. External temperatures can drop to low temperatures during these extended eclipse phases, requiring increased heating system activity to maintain module temperatures. The system's thermal storage units, particularly in Quetzal and Vicuña, play a crucial role during these periods by releasing stored heat to supplement electrical heating elements. Crew members conduct additional thermal system checks during MASA-OPS-006 (Crew Shift Handoff) to ensure all heating elements are functioning properly before extended eclipse periods.

Equinox periods (March and September) present the most stable thermal conditions, with relatively equal day-night cycles and moderate temperature fluctuations. However, these periods coincide with increased solar activity, requiring additional monitoring for potential radiation-related thermal effects. The station's thermal management system coordinates with MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory) during these periods, implementing additional cooling measures when solar particle events threaten to increase internal temperatures through radiation absorption.

## Maintenance Schedule for Thermal Components

The thermal management system follows a comprehensive maintenance schedule integrated with MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance). Weekly maintenance includes coolant level verification, filter inspections, and thermal sensor calibration across all modules. Monthly procedures involve more intensive tasks such as heat exchanger cleaning, radiator panel inspections, and redundant system testing. Engineer Kozlov oversees a rotating schedule where each module receives focused thermal system attention every six weeks, with Cóndor and Jaguar receiving priority due to their critical functions.

Quarterly maintenance includes coolant system flushing and replacement, thermal insulation inspections, and comprehensive system performance testing. These procedures are scheduled to coincide with resupply missions to minimize operational impact. The medical module Tucán receives additional monthly maintenance of its sterile air circulation system, while Quetzal's experimental cooling systems undergo bi-weekly performance verification to ensure research integrity.

Emergency maintenance procedures are activated whenever temperature deviations exceed medium-alert thresholds or when system performance degrades beyond specified parameters. These procedures include immediate component isolation, redundant system activation, and if necessary, module-specific evacuation protocols. All maintenance activities are logged in the station's technical database and transmitted to MASA ground control for analysis, with particular attention given to any trends indicating potential system degradation.

The thermal management system's maintenance schedule is designed to ensure high operational reliability, with redundant components allowing for continued operation during maintenance periods. Crew training includes regular drills for thermal system emergencies, with particular emphasis on rapid response to critical temperature deviations and coordination with MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) protocols when necessary.
