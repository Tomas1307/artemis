# MASA-DOC-020: EVA and External Operations Procedures

## MASA-OPS-004: EVA Preparation and Execution

Extravehicular activities (EVAs) are critical operations conducted outside the pressurized environment of Kuntur Station, requiring meticulous preparation and execution to ensure crew safety and mission success. MASA-OPS-004 governs all phases of EVA operations, from pre-deployment checks to post-EVA debriefings, with a standard duration of 360 minutes. This procedure is initiated as needed, typically for external maintenance, scientific instrument deployment, or structural inspections, and falls under the joint responsibility of Specialist 1 Kai Nakamura and Specialist 2 Fátima Al-Hassan, both assigned to the Quetzal module. The primary operational scope of MASA-OPS-004 encompasses the Jaguar and Vicuna modules, which house the airlock systems and external tooling required for EVA operations.

Preparation for an EVA begins well in advance of the scheduled operation, with a comprehensive review of structural integrity, life support systems, and communications infrastructure. The structural assessment ensures that the airlock seals and external handrails are secure, while life support checks verify the functionality of the Environmental Control and Life Support System (ECLSS) within the suits and modules. Communications systems are tested to confirm uninterrupted voice and telemetry links between the EVA crew, the Condor command center, and MASA ground stations. The engineer, Pavel Kozlov, oversees the technical readiness of the Jaguar module’s airlock, while the medical officer, Lucía Mendoza, conducts pre-EVA health evaluations of the crew to ensure physical readiness.

During the EVA, the crew operates in pairs, with one specialist designated as the lead and the other as the support. The lead specialist is responsible for executing the primary objectives of the EVA, while the support specialist monitors systems, manages tools, and assists with any unforeseen challenges. The Condor command center, under the supervision of Commander Santiago Reyes, maintains continuous communication with the EVA crew, providing real-time guidance and monitoring telemetry data. The pilot, Ana Valdivia, assists in coordinating the EVA from the Colibri module, ensuring that the station’s orientation and power systems remain stable throughout the operation.

Post-EVA procedures include a thorough debriefing session, where the crew reviews the execution of tasks, any anomalies encountered, and the performance of equipment. This session is documented and integrated into MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check) to ensure that any issues are addressed in subsequent maintenance cycles. The EVA suits and tools are then inspected, cleaned, and prepared for future use, with any necessary repairs or replacements logged in MASA-OPS-005 (Cargo Transfer and Inventory Update).

## Suit Systems Checklist

The EVA suit, designated as the MASA Extravehicular Mobility Unit (EMU), is a sophisticated system designed to protect crew members from the harsh conditions of space while providing mobility and life support. The suit systems checklist is a critical component of MASA-OPS-004, ensuring that all components are functional and ready for use. The checklist begins with a thorough inspection of the suit’s primary life support system, which includes the oxygen supply, CO2 scrubbing, and thermal regulation. The oxygen supply must be verified to provide a sufficient duration of breathable air, with a primary tank capacity of a substantial amount of oxygen and a secondary backup tank of a smaller amount. The CO2 scrubbing system is checked to ensure it can maintain levels below a safe threshold within the suit, preventing hypercapnia and ensuring crew comfort.

Thermal regulation is another critical aspect of the suit systems checklist. The EMU’s thermal control system must be capable of maintaining a stable internal temperature within a comfortable range, regardless of external conditions. This is achieved through a combination of active cooling loops and passive insulation, which are tested to ensure they can handle the extreme temperature fluctuations experienced in Low Earth Orbit. The suit’s communications system is also verified, with a primary radio frequency and a backup frequency, ensuring redundant communication pathways with the station and ground control.

The structural integrity of the suit is inspected for any signs of wear or damage, particularly in the joints and seals, which are critical for maintaining pressure and mobility. The suit’s pressure regulation system is tested to ensure it can maintain a stable internal pressure, with automatic adjustments to compensate for any minor leaks or fluctuations. The mobility joints, including the shoulders, elbows, and knees, are checked for smooth operation, ensuring that the crew can perform tasks without restriction. Finally, the suit’s emergency systems, including the backup oxygen supply and the emergency propulsion unit, are verified to ensure they are fully functional and ready for deployment in case of an emergency.

## Airlock Cycling Protocol

The airlock cycling protocol is a critical phase of MASA-OPS-004, ensuring the safe transition of crew members between the pressurized environment of the station and the vacuum of space. The protocol begins with the pre-depressurization phase, where the airlock is sealed and the internal pressure is gradually reduced to minimize the risk of decompression sickness. The airlock’s pressure is monitored in real-time, with a target depressurization rate, ensuring a controlled and safe transition. The crew members are secured within the airlock, with their suits fully pressurized and life support systems active.

Once the airlock pressure reaches a minimal level, the outer hatch is opened, and the crew members exit the station. The airlock is then repressurized to the station’s standard pressure, with a repressurization rate to ensure a smooth and safe transition. The airlock cycling protocol is closely monitored by the engineer, Pavel Kozlov, who oversees the technical aspects of the operation from the Jaguar module. In the event of an airlock seal failure, MASA-SEC-017 (Airlock Seal Failure Protocol) is activated, with a critical severity level and module-only scope, requiring immediate action to secure the airlock and prevent further pressure loss.

The airlock cycling protocol also includes contingency measures for rapid decompression, which can occur due to a sudden failure in the airlock seals or a breach in the suit. In such an event, MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) is activated, with a critical severity level and module-only scope, requiring the crew to immediately secure the airlock and initiate emergency procedures. The protocol emphasizes the importance of clear communication and coordination between the EVA crew, the airlock operator, and the command center to ensure a safe and efficient transition.

## Communication Requirements During EVA

Effective communication is essential for the success of any EVA, ensuring that the crew remains in constant contact with the station and ground control. The communication requirements during EVA are governed by MASA-OPS-004, with a primary focus on maintaining clear and uninterrupted voice and telemetry links. The EVA crew is equipped with dual communication systems, including a primary radio frequency and a backup frequency, ensuring redundant pathways for communication. The primary frequency is used for routine communications, while the backup frequency is activated in the event of interference or signal loss.

The Condor command center, under the supervision of Commander Santiago Reyes, serves as the central hub for EVA communications, coordinating between the EVA crew, the station’s modules, and MASA ground stations. The pilot, Ana Valdivia, assists in managing the communication systems from the Colibri module, ensuring that the station’s orientation and power systems remain stable throughout the operation. The communication protocol includes regular status updates, with the EVA crew providing position reports, suit telemetry, and task progress updates at regular intervals.

In the event of a communication blackout, MASA-SEC-007 (Communication Blackout Protocol) is activated, with a high severity level and station-wide scope, requiring immediate action to restore communication links. The protocol emphasizes the importance of maintaining clear and concise communication, with standardized phrases and procedures to ensure that critical information is conveyed accurately and efficiently. The EVA crew is trained to use hand signals and visual cues as backup communication methods, ensuring that tasks can be completed even in the event of a complete communication failure.

## Emergency Recall Procedures

Emergency recall procedures are a critical component of MASA-OPS-004, ensuring that the EVA crew can be safely and rapidly returned to the station in the event of an emergency. The procedures are activated in response to a variety of scenarios, including medical emergencies, suit malfunctions, or station-wide alerts such as radiation events or power failures. The emergency recall signal is initiated by the Condor command center, with a distinct audio tone and visual alert transmitted to the EVA crew’s communication systems.

Upon receiving the emergency recall signal, the EVA crew must immediately cease all activities and return to the airlock. The crew is trained to follow a predefined path back to the airlock, using handrails and tether points to ensure a safe and efficient return. The airlock is prepared for rapid repressurization, with the engineer, Pavel Kozlov, overseeing the technical aspects of the operation from the Jaguar module. The repressurization rate is increased to a rapid but safe level, ensuring a swift transition back to the station’s pressurized environment.

In the event of a medical emergency, MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) is activated, with a high severity level and module-only scope, requiring the medical officer, Lucía Mendoza, to prepare the Tucan module for immediate medical intervention. The emergency recall procedures also include contingency measures for suit malfunctions, with the crew trained to assist each other in the event of a suit breach or life support failure. The protocol emphasizes the importance of clear communication and coordination between the EVA crew, the airlock operator, and the command center to ensure a safe and efficient emergency recall.

The emergency recall procedures are regularly reviewed and practiced as part of MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill), ensuring that the crew is prepared to respond to any emergency scenario. The protocol emphasizes the importance of maintaining situational awareness and clear communication, with standardized phrases and procedures to ensure that critical information is conveyed accurately and efficiently. The emergency recall procedures are designed to minimize the risk to the EVA crew and ensure a safe and rapid return to the station in the event of an emergency.
