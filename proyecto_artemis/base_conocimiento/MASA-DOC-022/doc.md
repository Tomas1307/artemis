# MASA-DOC-022: Emergency Drills and Training Procedures

## MASA-OPS-015: Emergency Drill

The MASA-OPS-015 Emergency Drill represents the cornerstone of Kuntur Station's operational readiness, conducted bi-weekly across all modules to ensure crew proficiency in responding to the full spectrum of emergency protocols. Each drill spans 120 minutes, a duration carefully calibrated to allow for comprehensive scenario execution while maintaining operational efficiency. The bi-weekly frequency—approximately every 14 days—ensures that all six crew members maintain muscle memory for critical response actions without disrupting the station's scientific and maintenance schedules. Unlike routine procedures such as MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check), which focus on preventive measures, MASA-OPS-015 simulates active emergency conditions, requiring immediate decision-making under pressure.

The drill's scope encompasses all modules—Condor, Quetzal, Jaguar, Colibri, and Tucan—ensuring that crew members are prepared to respond to emergencies regardless of their assigned location. Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, responsible for Jaguar module operations, must demonstrate proficiency in both module-specific protocols like MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) and station-wide responses such as MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol). The drill's comprehensive nature means that even crew members primarily assigned to one module must be capable of responding to emergencies in any section of the station, reflecting the interconnected nature of Kuntur's life support and power systems.

Commander Santiago Reyes serves as the drill director, overseeing scenario execution and evaluating crew performance. This role is critical because the commander must coordinate responses across modules, ensuring that actions taken in one area do not compromise safety in another. For example, during a simulated MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure), the commander must verify that power rerouting in Condor does not inadvertently trigger a MASA-SEC-018 (Power Fluctuation Response) in Quetzal. The drill's structure includes a debrief phase where the commander identifies areas for improvement, particularly in inter-module communication and protocol handoffs.

The absence of a fixed UTC schedule for MASA-OPS-015 is intentional, allowing the commander to initiate drills at unpredictable times to simulate real emergency conditions. This unpredictability ensures that crew members cannot prepare for specific scenarios in advance, forcing them to rely on their training and the station's operational procedures. The drill's duration of 120 minutes is divided into three phases: scenario briefing, active response, and debrief. The active response phase is further segmented to allow for the simulation of multiple protocols, with transitions between scenarios designed to test the crew's adaptability.

## MASA-OPS-012: Crew Medical Examination

While MASA-OPS-015 focuses on emergency response, MASA-OPS-012 (Crew Medical Examination) ensures that all personnel maintain the physical and mental fitness required to execute those responses effectively. Conducted weekly in the Tucan module, each examination lasts 60 minutes and is performed by Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza. The weekly frequency is critical in the microgravity environment of Kuntur Station, where physiological changes can occur rapidly. Unlike emergency drills, which involve all crew members simultaneously, medical examinations are individual, allowing for focused assessment of each crew member's health status.

The scope of MASA-OPS-012 is limited to the Tucan module, where the station's medical equipment and life support systems are concentrated. This centralization ensures that Medical Officer Mendoza has access to all necessary diagnostic tools, including the station's advanced telemetry systems that monitor vital signs in real time. The examination includes a review of life support system data, particularly oxygen saturation levels and atmospheric pressure readings, to confirm that the crew member's physiological responses remain within nominal ranges. For example, if a crew member exhibits signs of hypoxia during the examination, Mendoza must determine whether the issue stems from individual health factors or a potential MASA-SEC-010 (Low Oxygen Warning Response) condition in their assigned module.

The absence of a fixed schedule for MASA-OPS-012 allows Mendoza to adjust examination timing based on operational demands. However, the weekly cadence is non-negotiable, as it aligns with MASA's medical protocols for long-duration spaceflight. Each examination includes a review of the crew member's exposure to radiation, with particular attention to cumulative doses that could approach the thresholds triggering MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol). The data collected during these examinations feeds into the station's medical database, which is cross-referenced with environmental data from other modules to identify potential correlations between health trends and station conditions.

## Drill Scenario Library

The effectiveness of MASA-OPS-015 depends on the diversity and realism of the scenarios simulated during each drill. The scenario library is structured to cover all active protocols, with particular emphasis on those with critical or high severity ratings. For example, MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) scenarios are designed to test the crew's ability to isolate affected modules while maintaining life support in adjacent sections. These drills often begin with a simulated pressure drop in a module, requiring Systems Engineer Kozlov to coordinate with Commander Reyes to execute the protocol's module-only scope without compromising the entire station.

Similarly, MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) scenarios are among the most complex, as they require immediate action to prevent catastrophic damage. During these drills, crew members must demonstrate proficiency in using fire suppression equipment while also managing the station's atmospheric control systems to prevent the spread of smoke or toxic gases. The scenario library includes variations where the fire originates in different modules, forcing the crew to adapt their response based on the specific equipment and layout of each area. For instance, a fire in the Quetzal module, where Specialist Kai Nakamura conducts experimental operations, requires different containment procedures than one in the Jaguar module, where power distribution systems are concentrated.

Station-wide protocols such as MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) and MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) are simulated less frequently but with greater intensity. These scenarios test the crew's ability to coordinate across all modules, with particular emphasis on communication protocols. During a radiation lockdown drill, Pilot Ana Valdivia must ensure that the Colibri module's navigation systems remain operational while the rest of the station implements shielding procedures. The scenario library includes variations where a primary protocol might be complicated by a secondary issue such as MASA-SEC-014 (CO2 Scrubber Failure Protocol), requiring the crew to prioritize responses based on severity and scope.

## Performance Evaluation Criteria

The evaluation of crew performance during MASA-OPS-015 drills is structured around three core metrics: response time, protocol adherence, and inter-module coordination. Response time is measured from the moment a protocol is triggered until the first corrective action is initiated. For critical-severity protocols like MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response), the expected response time is significantly shorter than for medium-severity protocols such as MASA-SEC-012 (Solar Flare Advisory). The evaluation criteria account for the physical distance between crew members and the affected module, with adjustments made for scenarios where the nearest qualified responder is not the primary assigned crew member.

Protocol adherence is evaluated based on the crew's ability to follow the exact steps outlined in each protocol document. For example, during a MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) scenario, Medical Officer Mendoza must be observed performing the correct sequence of diagnostic and treatment actions while also communicating with the commander to determine if module evacuation is necessary. The evaluation includes a review of the crew's use of checklists and their ability to adapt when unexpected complications arise, such as a secondary protocol being triggered mid-response.

Inter-module coordination is particularly critical for station-wide protocols and is evaluated through a review of communication logs and action timelines. During a MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) drill, the evaluation team assesses how effectively Engineer Kozlov communicates with Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan in the Quetzal module to ensure that experimental equipment is safely powered down before rerouting begins. The criteria also include an assessment of the commander's ability to maintain situational awareness across all modules, particularly when multiple protocols are active simultaneously.

## Medical Fitness Standards

The medical fitness standards for Kuntur Station crew members are designed to ensure that all personnel can perform their duties under both normal and emergency conditions. These standards are enforced through the weekly MASA-OPS-012 examinations and are divided into three categories: cardiovascular health, musculoskeletal integrity, and cognitive function. Cardiovascular health is particularly critical in microgravity, where the absence of gravitational stress can lead to rapid deconditioning. Crew members must maintain heart rates within a range that allows for the physical exertion required during emergency responses, such as rapidly moving between modules during a MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) scenario.

Musculoskeletal integrity is evaluated through a combination of assessments. The standards require that crew members be capable of performing tasks such as operating manual hatch controls in the event of a MASA-SEC-017 (Airlock Seal Failure Protocol) or maneuvering in restricted spaces while wearing emergency breathing apparatus. These physical requirements are particularly important for Engineer Kozlov and Specialist Nakamura, whose roles often require them to access tight equipment bays in the Jaguar and Quetzal modules.

Cognitive function standards ensure that crew members can process complex information under stress, a critical requirement for protocols like MASA-SEC-016 (Navigation Deviation Alert), where Pilot Valdivia must quickly analyze trajectory data and implement corrective actions. The standards include assessments of memory, problem-solving speed, and the ability to maintain focus during extended emergency scenarios. Medical Officer Mendoza uses a combination of tests and practical simulations to evaluate these cognitive metrics, with particular attention to any signs of fatigue or stress that could impair performance during a real emergency.

The integration of MASA-OPS-012 medical examinations with the emergency drill program ensures that fitness standards are not merely theoretical but are regularly tested under realistic conditions. For example, a crew member who demonstrates excellent cardiovascular health during a routine examination must also prove they can maintain that performance while executing the physical demands of a MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) response. This holistic approach to medical fitness and emergency preparedness is fundamental to Kuntur Station's operational philosophy, where the health of the crew and the safety of the station are inextricably linked.
