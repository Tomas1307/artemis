# MASA-DOC-010: Structural and Mechanical Safety Protocols

## Protocol Overview

The structural and mechanical safety protocols of Kuntur Station represent a critical layer of operational defense against the inherent risks of orbital habitation. At 408 km altitude, the station operates in an environment where micrometeorite impacts, thermal cycling, and mechanical stress from docking operations pose continuous threats to hull integrity and crew safety. These protocols are designed to detect, respond to, and mitigate structural anomalies before they escalate into catastrophic failures. The three primary protocols—MASA-SEC-008 (Structural Integrity Alert), MASA-SEC-013 (Docking Bay Pressure Anomaly), and MASA-SEC-017 (Airlock Seal Failure Protocol)—operate in tandem with the station's sensor network, which includes hull stress monitors, docking port pressure transducers, and airlock seal integrity sensors distributed across all six modules. Each protocol is triggered by specific threshold breaches that reflect engineering limits derived from ground-based stress testing and in-orbit operational experience. The response frameworks are tiered to match the severity of the detected anomaly, with high-severity alerts requiring immediate module-level action and critical-severity events demanding station-wide coordination. These protocols interface directly with MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check) and MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), ensuring that structural monitoring is both continuous and proactive.

## MASA-SEC-008: Structural Integrity Alert

MASA-SEC-008 (Structural Integrity Alert) activates when hull stress sensors detect readings exceeding 85% of rated capacity or when micrometeorite impact sensors register a positive event. The 85% threshold was established based on material fatigue analysis conducted during Kuntur's pre-launch certification, where aluminum-lithium alloy hull sections demonstrated nonlinear stress-strain behavior beyond this point. In operational terms, stress levels between 70% and 85% indicate elevated but manageable loads, often resulting from thermal expansion during solar exposure or minor debris impacts. When readings surpass 85%, the risk of microfracture propagation increases exponentially, necessitating immediate intervention. The protocol's high-severity classification reflects this urgency while maintaining a module-only scope to prevent unnecessary station-wide disruptions. Upon activation, the affected module's crew must inspect the flagged hull section using the module's internal diagnostic panel, which provides real-time stress mapping. Adjacent inter-module hatches are sealed as a precautionary measure to contain potential atmospheric loss, though full decompression is unlikely at this stage. A high-severity alert is automatically transmitted to the Cóndor command center, where Commander Santiago Reyes coordinates the response. If external inspection is required, Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov prepares the EVA team according to MASA-OPS-004 (EVA Preparation and Execution), with the Jaguar module serving as the primary staging area for repair operations. All sensor readings are documented in the station's maintenance log, cross-referenced with MASA-OPS-002 for trend analysis.

## MASA-SEC-013: Docking Bay Pressure Anomaly

The Vicuña module's docking bay represents one of Kuntur Station's most mechanically complex interfaces, where pressure differentials must be carefully managed to ensure safe vessel attachment. MASA-SEC-013 (Docking Bay Pressure Anomaly) triggers when the pressure differential across the docking port exceeds 5.0 kPa during active docking operations. This threshold was selected to balance operational efficiency with safety margins: differentials below 3.0 kPa are considered nominal, while readings between 3.0 kPa and 5.0 kPa indicate minor seal misalignment that can often be corrected through automated collar adjustments. Exceeding 5.0 kPa, however, suggests either a significant seal breach or improper docking sequence execution, both of which pose immediate risks to station integrity. The protocol's high-severity classification reflects the potential for rapid pressure equalization events that could compromise the docking bay's structural integrity. Upon activation, the docking sequence is aborted immediately, and the Vicuña module's inner hatch is sealed to prevent atmospheric contamination. Pilot Ana Valdivia, who oversees all docking operations from the Colibrí module, coordinates with the incoming vessel to maintain a safe holding position while the docking collar seals are inspected. The inspection process involves both internal pressure tests and, if necessary, external visual confirmation via the Vicuña module's external cameras. Only after the pressure differential returns to below 3.0 kPa and remains stable for a minimum of five minutes can docking operations resume, following the full procedures outlined in MASA-OPS-003 (Docking and Undocking Procedure).

## MASA-SEC-017: Airlock Seal Failure Protocol

Airlock operations are among the most critical mechanical procedures conducted on Kuntur Station, with seal integrity being the primary defense against catastrophic decompression. MASA-SEC-017 (Airlock Seal Failure Protocol) represents the station's most severe module-level mechanical alert, activating under two conditions: when an airlock door fails its automated pressure test or when the pressure equalization rate exceeds 0.5 kPa per minute. The 0.5 kPa/min threshold was established through ground-based testing that demonstrated seal degradation becomes irreversible at this rate, with full failure typically occurring within a rapid timeframe if unchecked. This critical-severity protocol demands immediate action to prevent crew exposure to vacuum conditions. If an EVA is in progress when the protocol triggers, the crew must abort the operation immediately and return to the airlock's inner chamber. Under no circumstances should the outer airlock door be opened once the protocol is active, as this could lead to uncontrolled decompression. The inner hatch is sealed automatically upon protocol activation, and a critical alert is transmitted station-wide, with Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza preparing the Tucán module's medical bay for potential decompression injuries. The affected airlock is taken offline until emergency seal inspection and replacement can be conducted, typically requiring a significant repair time and full recertification before returning to operational status. This protocol interfaces directly with MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response), which would activate if seal failure leads to actual pressure loss.

## Hull Inspection Procedures

Routine hull inspections form the backbone of Kuntur Station's preventive maintenance strategy, complementing the automated monitoring provided by the structural integrity protocols. These inspections are conducted in accordance with MASA-OPS-002 (Scheduled Preventive Maintenance), with full external surveys performed at regular intervals and focused inspections following any MASA-SEC-008 activation. The inspection process begins with a comprehensive review of hull stress sensor data, where readings are analyzed for trends indicating localized material fatigue. Particular attention is paid to areas around docking ports and solar array attachment points, where stress concentrations are most likely to develop. External inspections are conducted via EVA, with crews using the station's robotic inspection arms to access hard-to-reach areas. The inspection checklist includes visual examination for micrometeorite impact craters, measurement of any detected deformations, and testing of suspect welds. Any anomalies detected during these inspections are documented and prioritized based on their potential to affect structural integrity. For example, impact craters or weld anomalies trigger immediate follow-up under MASA-SEC-008 protocols. The inspection data is cross-referenced with historical trends to identify modules requiring more frequent monitoring, with the Jaguar module typically receiving additional scrutiny due to its role as the primary life support hub.

## EVA Repair Team Composition

Extravehicular Activity (EVA) repair teams are assembled based on the specific requirements of the structural anomaly being addressed, with team composition following the guidelines established in MASA-OPS-004 (EVA Preparation and Execution). For standard hull inspections and minor repairs, the team consists of two crew members: Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov, who serves as the primary repair specialist, and either Specialist Kai Nakamura or Specialist Fátima Al-Hassan, who provide secondary support and documentation. This configuration ensures that at least one crew member with specialized engineering training is always present during external operations. For more complex repairs, such as those requiring weld reinforcement or seal replacement, the team is expanded to include Commander Santiago Reyes, who oversees the operation from the Cóndor module's command center. Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza remains on standby in the Tucán module during all EVA operations, prepared to implement MASA-SEC-005 (Medical Emergency Response) if decompression injuries occur. The EVA team composition is finalized during the pre-EVA briefing, where the specific repair procedures are reviewed, tool requirements are confirmed, and contingency plans are established. All EVA operations are conducted with redundant safety tethers and continuous real-time telemetry monitoring to ensure crew safety during these high-risk procedures.
