# MASA-DOC-015: Life Support Systems Comprehensive Guide

## O2 Generation and Distribution

The Jaguar module serves as the life support backbone of Kuntur Station, housing the primary oxygen generation system that maintains breathable atmosphere across all modules. Oxygen levels within Jaguar are maintained between 19.5% and 23.5% under normal operating conditions, with the system automatically adjusting electrolysis rates to compensate for crew metabolic consumption and minor leaks. The lower normal threshold of 19.5% ensures adequate partial pressure for crew cognition and physical performance, while the upper limit of 23.5% prevents excessive flammability risks in the station's mixed-material environment.

When oxygen concentrations drop below 18.0%, the system triggers a low-severity alert, indicating potential inefficiencies in the electrolysis stack or minor distribution leaks. This condition requires immediate diagnostic checks as part of MASA-OPS-010 (Life Support Maintenance), with particular attention to the redundant oxygen storage tanks that maintain a significant backup supply. Should levels continue to decline below 16.0%, a medium-severity condition is declared, necessitating activation of secondary oxygen generation systems and implementation of MASA-SEC-010 (Low Oxygen Warning Response). The critical threshold of 14.0% oxygen triggers MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response), which includes automatic module isolation and crew evacuation to the Condor command center where Commander Santiago Reyes coordinates emergency procedures.

The oxygen distribution network operates at a nominal pressure of 101.3 kPa, with Jaguar's pressure maintained between 99.0 kPa and 103.0 kPa to ensure consistent gas exchange across module interfaces. Pressure drops below 95.0 kPa initiate low-severity alerts, while readings below 90.0 kPa constitute medium-severity conditions requiring immediate pressure integrity checks. The critical threshold of 85.0 kPa activates MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response), with automatic sealing of module hatchways and deployment of emergency oxygen masks throughout the affected module.

## CO2 Scrubbing System

Carbon dioxide removal represents the most metabolically sensitive component of Kuntur's life support architecture. The Jaguar module's lithium hydroxide scrubbers maintain CO2 concentrations below a threshold under normal conditions, with the system's dual-bed design allowing continuous operation during regeneration cycles. CO2 levels between thresholds trigger low-severity alerts, typically indicating partial scrubber bed saturation or minor airflow restrictions in the ventilation system. This condition requires immediate bed regeneration and airflow diagnostics as specified in MASA-OPS-010.

Should CO2 concentrations exceed a threshold but remain below a threshold, a medium-severity condition is declared, necessitating activation of the secondary scrubber system and implementation of MASA-SEC-014 (CO2 Scrubber Failure Protocol). The critical threshold of a CO2 concentration triggers station-wide alerts and potential evacuation to the Condor module, where the command center's independent life support system can sustain the full crew for an extended period. This threshold reflects the physiological limit where crew members begin experiencing significant cognitive impairment and increased heart rate, compromising operational decision-making capabilities.

The CO2 scrubbing system's power consumption represents a critical load on Jaguar's electrical bus, with the scrubbers drawing power during normal operation. Power fluctuations above a percentage of rated capacity trigger low-severity alerts, while sustained operation above a percentage constitutes a medium-severity condition requiring immediate load shedding of non-critical systems. The critical power threshold of a percentage activates MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure), with automatic transfer to backup power systems and potential station-wide power conservation measures.

## Water Recycling and Purification

Kuntur Station's water recycling system processes all wastewater through a multi-stage filtration and purification process, with the Jaguar module housing the primary distillation and catalytic oxidation units. The system maintains a closed-loop efficiency, recovering potable water per crew member daily from urine, condensate, and hygiene wastewater. This recovery rate ensures the station's water storage capacity remains sufficient for contingency operations without resupply.

Water quality monitoring occurs continuously, with conductivity sensors triggering alerts when total dissolved solids exceed a threshold. This threshold indicates potential membrane degradation in the reverse osmosis system, requiring immediate maintenance under MASA-OPS-010. Should conductivity exceed a threshold, the system automatically diverts water to the contingency storage tanks while activating MASA-SEC-015 (Water Recycling System Failure). The critical threshold of a threshold triggers complete system shutdown and transition to emergency water rations, with Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza coordinating hydration protocols to prevent dehydration among the crew.

The water recycling system's thermal management represents a heat load on Jaguar's cooling systems, with the distillation process generating waste heat. This thermal load contributes to the module's temperature profile, with normal operating temperatures maintained between 19.0°C and 23.0°C. Temperature excursions above 28.0°C trigger low-severity alerts, while sustained operation above 38.0°C constitutes a medium-severity condition requiring immediate cooling loop diagnostics. The critical temperature threshold of 50.0°C activates MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression), with automatic shutdown of non-essential systems and deployment of the module's halon fire suppression system.

## Per-Module Oxygen Operational Ranges

All habitable modules on Kuntur Station maintain identical oxygen operational ranges, reflecting the station's integrated life support architecture. The Condor command center, Quetzal research laboratory, and Tucan medical module each maintain oxygen concentrations between 19.5% and 23.5% under normal conditions, with identical alert thresholds to those in the Jaguar module. This standardization enables seamless crew rotation and emergency evacuation between modules without requiring atmospheric acclimatization.

The Colibri module, while primarily serving as the station's docking and storage hub, maintains the same oxygen operational ranges when pressurized for crew access. During docking operations, oxygen levels are monitored particularly closely, with any deviation below 18.0% triggering immediate implementation of MASA-OPS-003 (Docking and Undocking Procedure) abort protocols. The Vicuña module, serving as the station's primary storage and logistics hub, maintains atmospheric conditions identical to other modules when crew access is required, though it typically operates in a low-power, unpressurized state during normal operations.

This standardized oxygen profile across all modules enables the implementation of station-wide life support protocols, with MASA-SEC-014 (CO2 Scrubber Failure Protocol) and MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response) applying uniformly regardless of the affected module. The uniformity also simplifies crew training and emergency response procedures, as all personnel can respond appropriately to life support alerts regardless of their current module assignment.

## Humidity Control Across All Modules

Humidity control represents a critical factor in both crew comfort and equipment reliability across Kuntur Station. The Jaguar module maintains relative humidity between 40% and 60% under normal conditions, with this range optimized to prevent both microbial growth and static electricity buildup in the station's electronic systems. Humidity levels between 30-39% or 61-70% trigger low-severity alerts, typically indicating minor condensate system inefficiencies or temporary crew activity spikes.

Should humidity fall between 20-29% or rise between 71-80%, medium-severity alerts are triggered, requiring immediate adjustment of the condensate recovery system and potential activation of auxiliary humidification or dehumidification units. The critical thresholds of below 15% or above 90% humidity activate station-wide environmental alerts, with potential impacts on both crew health and equipment operation. Prolonged exposure to humidity below 15% can cause respiratory irritation and increased static discharge risks, while humidity above 90% creates ideal conditions for fungal growth and corrosion in electronic systems.

The humidity control system interacts closely with the station's thermal management, as condensate recovery from the atmosphere represents a heat transfer mechanism. During periods of high crew activity or equipment operation, the humidity control system can remove water from the atmosphere, contributing to the overall water recovery budget. This integration requires careful coordination between the life support and thermal control systems, with Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov responsible for daily monitoring of these interactions as part of MASA-OPS-001 (Daily System Health Check).

## Air Quality Monitoring Sensors

Kuntur Station employs a distributed network of air quality sensors, with primary monitoring nodes located in each module's environmental control system. The Jaguar module houses the central air quality monitoring system, which samples atmospheric conditions and provides real-time telemetry to both the Condor command center and MASA ground stations. Each sensor node measures oxygen concentration, CO2 levels, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), particulate matter, and microbial content.

The VOC monitoring system triggers alerts when concentrations exceed a threshold, with this threshold representing the detection limit for common spacecraft off-gassing compounds. Should VOC levels exceed a threshold, a medium-severity alert is triggered, requiring immediate activation of the station's charcoal filtration system and implementation of MASA-SEC-009 (Toxic Atmosphere Detection). The critical threshold of a VOC concentration activates station-wide ventilation protocols and potential crew evacuation to the Condor module.

Particulate monitoring occurs continuously, with the system triggering alerts when particle counts exceed a threshold. This threshold indicates potential filter degradation or unusual dust generation from equipment operation. Should particle counts exceed a threshold, a medium-severity condition is declared, requiring immediate filter replacement and source investigation. The critical threshold of a threshold triggers implementation of MASA-SEC-019 (Biological Contamination Containment), with potential module isolation and decontamination procedures.

## Filter Replacement Schedule

The life support system's filtration components follow a strict replacement schedule to ensure continuous operation and air quality maintenance. The Jaguar module's primary HEPA filters require replacement under normal operating conditions, with this interval reduced during periods of high particulate generation such as extensive equipment maintenance or cargo transfer operations. The activated charcoal filters in the VOC scrubbing system have a nominal lifespan, though this can be significantly reduced during periods of elevated off-gassing from new equipment or materials.

The CO2 scrubber's lithium hydroxide canisters require replacement under normal crew metabolic loads, with the station maintaining an adequate number of spare canisters at all times. This replacement schedule ensures that the scrubbing system can handle the station's maximum crew complement of six personnel without risk of saturation. The water recycling system's reverse osmosis membranes have a nominal lifespan, though this can be extended with proper maintenance and cleaning cycles as specified in MASA-OPS-010.

All filter replacements are logged in the station's maintenance database and cross-referenced with the inventory management system to ensure adequate spare parts availability. The replacement schedule is coordinated with the station's resupply missions, with critical filtration components prioritized in the cargo manifest. During filter replacement operations, the affected systems are temporarily bypassed or operated in redundant mode to maintain continuous life support functionality, with the entire process typically completed depending on the specific component being replaced.
