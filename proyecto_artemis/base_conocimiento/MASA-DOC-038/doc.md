# MASA-DOC-038: Emergency Quick-Reference Card

## Critical Protocol Summary Table

The MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) protocol is triggered when module pressure drops below 85.0 kPa, a threshold chosen to prevent hypoxia and structural stress. This critical-level protocol requires immediate sealing of adjacent hatches, evacuation of affected personnel, and activation of backup pressure systems. The 85.0 kPa threshold represents the minimum safe pressure for sustained human activity without risk of altitude sickness or equipment malfunction. When triggered, the protocol's module-only scope ensures containment while allowing unaffected areas to maintain normal operations.

MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response) activates when oxygen concentration falls below 14.0%, the minimum level required to sustain human consciousness and cognitive function. This critical-level protocol mandates immediate evacuation of the affected module and activation of emergency oxygen reserves from the Jaguar module. The 14.0% threshold was established based on physiological studies showing rapid cognitive impairment below this level, with potential loss of consciousness occurring within minutes. The protocol's module-only scope prevents cross-contamination while ensuring crew safety.

MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) triggers when either fire sensors detect combustion or when temperature rises exceed 2.0°C per minute, indicating potential thermal runaway conditions. This critical-level protocol requires immediate activation of fire suppression systems and evacuation of affected personnel. The 2.0°C per minute threshold was selected as it represents the minimum rate at which most materials begin to degrade or combust in the station's oxygen-rich environment. The protocol's module-only scope contains the threat while protecting adjacent modules.

MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) activates station-wide when radiation levels exceed 5.0 mSv/hour, the threshold at which acute radiation syndrome becomes a significant risk during prolonged exposure. This critical-level protocol requires all crew to relocate to shielded modules (Jaguar and Cóndor) and cancellation of all EVA operations. The 5.0 mSv/hour threshold was established based on international space medicine standards for short-term exposure limits. The station-wide scope reflects the pervasive nature of radiation threats in low Earth orbit.

MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) triggers when either total power consumption exceeds 97% of rated capacity or when main bus voltage drops by more than 15%. This critical-level protocol requires immediate load shedding of non-essential systems and activation of backup power. The 97% threshold represents the point at which power distribution systems begin to experience instability, while the 15% voltage drop indicates potential system failure. The station-wide scope reflects the interconnected nature of Kuntur Station's power grid.

MASA-SEC-020 (Emergency Station Evacuation) activates when either two or more critical systems fail simultaneously or when a catastrophic structural breach is confirmed. This critical-level protocol requires immediate evacuation to emergency capsules and transmission of a MAYDAY signal through the Colibrí module's communication systems. The two-system failure threshold was established based on redundancy analysis showing that simultaneous failures in multiple critical systems exceed the station's backup capabilities. The station-wide scope and immediate evacuation requirements reflect the existential threat posed by these conditions.

This route was designed to provide the shortest path to safety while avoiding potential bottlenecks at the Colibrí communication hub. The Cóndor module's central location makes it the natural assembly point for station-wide evacuations, with Commander Santiago Reyes responsible for coordinating the evacuation sequence as specified in MASA-OPS-015 (Emergency Drill).

For the Quetzal research module, evacuation routes prioritize access to both the emergency capsules and the shielded Jaguar module. The primary route exits through the southern hatch to the central corridor, while the secondary route connects through the Vicuña module's western access point. This dual-path design accounts for potential blockages in the central corridor during major incidents. Research specialists Kai Nakamura and Fátima Al-Hassan are trained to guide personnel through these routes during emergencies, with particular attention to securing ongoing experiments when time permits.

This configuration reflects Jaguar's critical role in life support and its proximity to shielded areas. Systems Engineer Pavel Kozlov maintains primary responsibility for ensuring these routes remain clear during emergencies, with particular attention to the module's oxygen reserves which may be required during evacuations.

The primary route leads to the emergency capsules through the central corridor, while the secondary route connects to the Cóndor module's western hatch. The Vicuña storage module's evacuation routes are designed to accommodate potential cargo shifts during emergencies. The primary route exits through the eastern hatch to the central corridor, while the secondary route connects to the Quetzal module's western access point. The Tucán medical module features direct access to emergency capsules through its southern hatch, with secondary evacuation through the Cóndor module's eastern connection. This configuration reflects the module's critical medical functions and the need for rapid evacuation of patients. Medical Officer Lucía Mendoza maintains primary responsibility for coordinating medical evacuations, ensuring that patients are stabilized and transported according to MASA-OPS-012 (Crew Medical Examination) protocols.

## Emergency Contact Frequencies

The primary emergency communication frequency for Kuntur Station is 437.800 MHz, designated for direct voice contact with MASA Mission Control in Quito, Ecuador. This UHF frequency was selected for its reliability in low Earth orbit communications and its compatibility with international space rescue protocols. The frequency operates with a standard deviation and supports both analog voice and digital telemetry transmission, ensuring redundancy in emergency situations.

Secondary emergency communications utilize the 243.000 MHz frequency, designated for international space rescue coordination through the COSPAS-SARSAT system. This frequency provides global coverage and interoperability with other space agencies' rescue assets. The 243.000 MHz channel operates with a standard bandwidth and supports both distress beacon transmissions and voice communications during search and rescue operations.

For medical emergencies requiring specialized consultation, the station maintains a dedicated 435.100 MHz frequency for direct communication with MASA's medical support team at the Simón Bolívar Andean Medicine Institute in La Paz. This frequency operates with a standard deviation and supports encrypted digital transmission of medical telemetry data, including vital signs and diagnostic images. The frequency was selected to avoid interference with primary communication channels while providing sufficient bandwidth for medical data transmission.

Inter-module emergency communications utilize the 433.920 MHz frequency, designated for internal station communications during critical incidents. This frequency supports full-duplex operations with a standard channel spacing, allowing simultaneous transmission between all modules. The frequency was chosen for its propagation characteristics within the station's metallic structure and its compatibility with the station's internal communication systems.

For direct communication with emergency response capsules, the station uses the 436.500 MHz frequency, designated for capsule-station communications during evacuation scenarios. This frequency operates with a standard deviation and supports both voice and telemetry transmission, including capsule system status and crew vital signs. The frequency was selected to provide reliable communication during the critical separation and descent phases of emergency evacuations.

## First Response Actions by Protocol Type

For MASA-SEC-001 (Rapid Decompression Response) events, the first responder must immediately seal all adjacent inter-module hatches to prevent pressure loss propagation. The affected module's hatch controls should be locked in the closed position using the manual override switches located at each hatch. Simultaneously, the responder should activate the module's backup pressure systems by engaging the emergency pressurization controls in the module's environmental control panel. The responder must then initiate a critical alert transmission through the module's communication terminal, ensuring the signal is sent on both primary and secondary frequencies. All crew members in the affected module must don emergency oxygen masks from the wall-mounted stations before evacuating through the predetermined routes.

During MASA-SEC-002 (Oxygen Depletion Response) events, the first responder must immediately activate the emergency oxygen supply from the Jaguar module's reserves using the cross-module oxygen distribution controls. The responder should verify oxygen flow to the affected module through the digital flow meters in the environmental control panel. Simultaneously, the responder must initiate module evacuation, ensuring all crew members have activated their personal oxygen supplies. The responder should then seal the affected module using the hatch control panel, preventing oxygen-depleted air from contaminating adjacent modules. A critical alert must be transmitted through the module's communication system, with particular attention to confirming signal reception by the Cóndor command module.

For MASA-SEC-003 (Fire and Thermal Runaway Suppression) events, the first responder must immediately activate the module's fire suppression system using the red-activated control panels located at each hatch. The responder should then cut ventilation to the affected module by engaging the emergency ventilation shutdown switches in the environmental control panel. All crew members must evacuate immediately through the nearest safe route, with the responder ensuring no personnel remain in the module. The responder should then seal the module using the hatch controls and initiate a critical alert transmission. Particular attention should be paid to monitoring adjacent modules for temperature increases through the thermal monitoring system.

During MASA-SEC-004 (Radiation Lockdown Protocol) events, the first responder must immediately initiate station-wide alerts through the central communication system, ensuring all crew members are aware of the radiation threat. The responder should then verify that all crew have relocated to the shielded Jaguar and Cóndor modules by checking the personnel location system. All EVA operations must be canceled through the airlock control systems, with particular attention to ensuring no crew members are outside the station. The responder should activate the station's radiation shielding protocols by engaging the electromagnetic shielding controls in the Cóndor module. Continuous monitoring of individual radiation exposure levels should be initiated through the crew health monitoring system.

For MASA-SEC-006 (Power Grid Critical Failure) events, the first responder must immediately shed all non-essential loads by engaging the emergency power management controls in the Cóndor module's power distribution panel. The responder should verify that life support and communication systems remain powered by checking the critical systems status display. Backup power systems should be activated using the manual override switches in the power control center. A station-wide critical alert should be transmitted, with particular attention to confirming reception by MASA ground control. The responder should then initiate power restriction protocols, ensuring only essential systems remain operational.

During MASA-SEC-020 (Emergency Station Evacuation) events, the first responder must immediately initiate the all-crew evacuation sequence by activating the emergency capsule preparation systems. The responder should verify that all crew members have donned emergency suits by checking the suit status indicators in the personnel monitoring system. Mission-critical data should be secured to portable storage devices using the emergency data transfer protocols. The responder should then assist Commander Santiago Reyes in coordinating the evacuation sequence, ensuring that the medical officer, Lucía Mendoza, is the last to leave the station. A MAYDAY signal should be transmitted through the Colibrí module's communication systems on all emergency frequencies.
